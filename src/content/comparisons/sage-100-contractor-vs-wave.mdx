# Sage 100 Contractor vs Wave: Accounting Fit

Sage 100 Contractor and Wave can both appear in an accounting-software search, but they are not close substitutes. Sage 100 Contractor is a construction accounting and business-management system for contractors that need job cost, project detail, office controls, and a guided quote process. Wave is a small-business bookkeeping tool for owners who need estimates, invoices, bills, and basic records before they are ready for a heavier accounting stack.

## The buying split

### Sage is for accounting-system change

Sage's official product page says Sage 100 Contractor manages all phases of construction and service management and gives small to mid-sized contractors access to critical business and project information. It also points buyers toward request-info, watch-demo, and talk-to-an-expert paths. That matters because Sage is not trying to be a quick invoice app. It is trying to become the accounting and business-management system that organizes job cost, project status, estimating, budgets, proposals, subcontracts, purchase orders, dashboards, and reports.

Atlas verifies Sage 100 Contractor as custom quote only. The official Sage page does not publish a public monthly, annual, implementation, or license price table for Sage 100 Contractor. That is not a minor detail. A contractor should evaluate Sage with a written scope that separates software, users, modules, deployment, implementation, training, support, and renewal terms.

### Wave is for simple books before complexity arrives

Wave's official pricing page lists Starter at $0 and Pro at $19 USD per month or $190 USD per year. Starter includes unlimited estimates, invoices, bills, and bookkeeping records. Pro adds the automation pieces that make bookkeeping less manual, including bank transaction imports, transaction categorization, receipt capture, late-payment reminders, extra account users, integrations, and stronger support access.

That makes Wave useful for a contractor who is still early, solo, or invoice-light. It helps an owner stop mixing paperwork, receipts, and bank records without committing to a construction accounting implementation. The catch is simple: Wave is not a contractor job-costing platform. It can organize small-business books, but it will not replace construction accounting controls for a company that needs WIP reporting, AIA billing, certified payroll, lien waivers, or job-cost structure by phase.

## Pricing reality

Sage is the harder product to budget before a call. Atlas and the official Sage source both support quote-only language: request information, watch a demo, talk to an expert, and scope the system around the contractor's needs. Do not compare Sage against Wave as if both vendors publish the same kind of checkout price. Sage's cost only becomes meaningful after the buyer confirms the implementation model, users, modules, hosting or deployment approach, reporting needs, support plan, and training scope.

Wave is transparent at the software-subscription layer. Starter is $0, and Pro is $19 per month or $190 per year. The official page also shows payment processing, receipt, payroll, bookkeeping, and advisor add-ons, so a contractor should still budget beyond the subscription if those services matter. But the core accounting-software decision is simple enough to model before signup.

The practical comparison is not "cheap versus expensive." It is "starter bookkeeping versus construction accounting." Wave is cheaper because it solves a smaller problem. Sage is heavier because the buyer is solving job cost and contractor financial control, not just invoice records.

## Where Sage 100 Contractor wins

### Construction accounting depth

Sage's strongest fit is a contractor that needs financial controls tied to the way jobs are actually run. The official page describes access to critical job cost and project details, customizable dashboards, industry-specific reports, integrated estimating, and exported budgets, proposals, subcontracts, and purchase orders. For a contractor whose owner, controller, or accountant needs job-level truth, those are core buying reasons.

### Certified payroll, AIA billing, and lien waivers

The official Sage page specifically cites certified payroll, AIA billings, and lien waivers as complex construction office issues Sage 100 Contractor can manage in one tool. Those workflows are not small-business bookkeeping features. They are contractor accounting controls that become important on commercial, prevailing-wage, progress-billing, and compliance-heavy work.

### A guided evaluation can protect the buyer

Custom quote pricing is frustrating when a buyer wants a fast number. It can still be useful when the software decision depends on chart of accounts, job-cost structure, reporting requirements, users, data migration, training, and support. Sage should be evaluated with the accounting team in the room, not as an impulse software trial.

## Where Wave wins

### The $0 starting point is real

Wave Starter gives very small contractors a real starting point for invoices and bookkeeping records without a monthly software subscription. That can be the right first move for an owner who is still proving volume, sending basic invoices, and trying to keep tax-time records cleaner.

### Simple invoicing and bookkeeping are easier to adopt

A solo contractor does not always need a construction accounting implementation. Sometimes the urgent problem is getting estimates, invoices, bills, payments, and basic records into one place. Wave fits that stage better than Sage because the setup is self-serve and the workflow stays focused on simple books.

### Pro creates a clear upgrade path

Wave Pro is the plan to consider when manual bookkeeping starts costing time. The official pricing page lists auto-imported bank transactions, transaction categorization, unlimited receipt capture, payment reminders, additional users, integrations, and dashboard reports on Pro. That is useful when the owner wants automation without changing the whole accounting system.

## Where each product is the wrong choice

Sage 100 Contractor is the wrong choice if the business only needs basic invoices, bill records, and tax-time organization. The product deserves a serious demo, but it is overkill for a solo contractor who is still deciding whether paid bookkeeping software is necessary at all. If the owner cannot name a construction accounting problem, Sage is likely too much system.

Wave is the wrong choice if job-cost accuracy is the reason for the search. It is not built for construction accounting, WIP reporting, certified payroll, AIA billing, lien waiver workflows, or detailed project financial controls. If the owner needs to know job margin by phase before month end, Wave will not be enough.

Both products can be wrong if the real pain is field operations instead of accounting. Scheduling, dispatch, customer communication, daily logs, and project coordination may point toward Jobber, Housecall Pro, Knowify, Buildertrend, or another operations platform. Use this comparison only if the core decision is accounting depth.

## Evaluation plan

### Test Sage with an accounting scenario

Bring one real job to the Sage demo. Ask how the system handles estimating, budget export, job cost, purchase orders, subcontract workflows, certified payroll, AIA billing, lien waivers, dashboards, and industry-specific reports. Then ask for a written quote that separates software, users, modules, deployment, implementation, training, support, and renewal terms.

Do not accept a generic demo. The value of Sage depends on whether it can support the company's actual accounting structure. The accountant or controller should help define the test before the sales call.

### Test Wave with one full month of simple books

For Wave, test the work a solo contractor actually does in a month: create an estimate, send an invoice, record a payment, enter bills, connect bookkeeping records, and check whether the reports are enough for tax prep and owner visibility. If manual entry feels painful, compare Pro rather than assuming Starter is the final state.

The decision point is whether the owner is trying to become more organized or trying to replace a weak accounting backbone. Wave helps with the first problem. Sage is for the second.

## FAQ

### Is Sage 100 Contractor better than Wave for contractors?

Sage 100 Contractor is better for contractors that need construction accounting, job cost detail, certified payroll, AIA billing, lien waivers, dashboards, and reports. Wave is better for solo contractors or tiny businesses that only need starter invoices, bills, and bookkeeping records.

### Which is cheaper, Sage 100 Contractor or Wave?

Wave is easier to budget because Atlas verifies Starter at $0 and Pro at $19 per month or $190 per year. Sage 100 Contractor is custom quote only, so buyers need a written scope from Sage or an authorized expert before comparing total cost.

### Can Wave handle construction job costing?

Wave can keep basic books, estimates, invoices, bills, and bookkeeping records. It should not be treated as construction job-costing software. Contractors that need phase-level cost control, WIP reporting, AIA billing, or certified payroll should evaluate Sage 100 Contractor or another construction accounting system.

### Does Sage 100 Contractor publish pricing?

No public plan table was found in Atlas or on the official Sage 100 Contractor product page. Sage routes buyers to request information, watch a demo, call, or talk to an expert. Use quote-only language unless a current official Sage quote backs a specific number.

### Who should start with Wave?

Wave fits solo contractors, side businesses, and very small trade companies that need simple paperwork and cleaner records before they need formal accounting controls. It is most useful when the owner values a $0 starting point and can live without construction-specific reporting.
