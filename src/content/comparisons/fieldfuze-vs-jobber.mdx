# FieldFuze vs Jobber: Field Service Software Compared

<p>FieldFuze and Jobber are both field service management platforms, but they are built for different buyers. Jobber is the default starting point for solo operators and small service teams that want scheduling, quoting, invoicing, and a polished mobile app. FieldFuze is the value challenger for growing crews that need included seats, inventory management, contract tools, and QuickBooks without per-user add-on costs.</p>

<p><strong>Disclosure:</strong> Some links on this page are affiliate links. ContractorSoftwareHub may earn a commission if you sign up through FieldFuze or Jobber links. The recommendation is based on fit, not payout.</p>

<p><strong>Short verdict:</strong> Start with Jobber if you are a solo operator or a small team that wants to test the product before buying. Switch your attention to FieldFuze when your crew has 5-15 users, you need inventory and contract tools, and included seats matter more than a free trial.</p>

## The team-size test

### FieldFuze is built around crew pricing, not single-user pricing

<p>FieldFuze's pricing page is unusually clear for field service software. Core is $49/mo with 3 seats. Pro is $349/mo with 15 seats included, and additional seats are $8.99 each. Enterprise is $799/mo with 25 seats. No setup fees, no annual contracts, and the company says most contractors are live within 24 hours.</p>

<p>That structure changes the conversation for a growing crew. A 10-person team on FieldFuze Pro pays $349/mo flat, which works out to about $34.90 per person before payment processing. A 15-person crew pays the same $349/mo, or about $23.27 per person. The math gets more attractive as headcount grows because the price does not scale per user the way Jobber's does.</p>

<p>The tradeoff is the evaluation path. FieldFuze does not offer a self-serve free trial. Buyers book a demo or talk to sales. That is appropriate for a product targeting crews that need an operational platform, but it makes the buying process heavier than clicking "start free trial" on Jobber.</p>

### Jobber is built for clean daily execution

<p>Jobber is narrower, and for many small shops that is the point. It helps a solo operator or small service team handle the daily workflow: schedule the job, send the quote, get approval, dispatch the tech, send the invoice, collect payment, and keep the customer informed. The mobile app is consistently praised across Capterra and G2 reviews, and the 14-day free trial with no credit card makes it easy to test before committing.</p>

<p>Jobber publishes its pricing in a way that is easy to model. Core starts at $29/mo annual or $49/mo monthly for one user. Connect starts at $99/mo annual for one user or $149/mo annual for the 5-user team version. Grow starts at $149/mo annual for one user or $299/mo annual for the 10-user team. Plus starts at $529/mo annual with 15 users included. Additional users are $29/mo each after plan limits. The <a href="/jobber-pricing/">Jobber pricing guide</a> has the full plan comparison.</p>

<p>For a solo operator or a team of 2-3, Jobber is usually the lower-risk and lower-cost starting point. The question is what happens when the crew grows past 5 users and needs inventory, contracts, or other operational tools that Jobber does not natively bundle.</p>

## Pricing and seat math

### FieldFuze Pro vs Jobber team plans

<p>The real pricing comparison is not FieldFuze Core vs Jobber Core. It is FieldFuze Pro vs Jobber's team-level plans, because that is where most growing crews end up.</p>

<p>For a 10-person crew, FieldFuze Pro at $349/mo flat beats Jobber Grow team at $299/mo annual, but FieldFuze includes inventory, contract management, e-signatures, and change orders that Jobber does not natively offer. If your crew needs those tools, the $50/mo difference buys real operational depth.</p>

<p>For a 15-person crew, FieldFuze Pro at $349/mo flat with 15 included seats compares against Jobber Plus at $529/mo annual with 15 users. That is a $180/mo difference, and FieldFuze still includes inventory, contracts, and other tools that Jobber either lacks or requires add-ons for. Jobber counters with a larger review base, a proven mobile app, and the Marketing Suite, AI Receptionist, and Pipeline add-ons available on Plus.</p>

### Payment processing rates

<p>FieldFuze processes payments through Stripe Connect with tiered rates: 2.9% on Core, 2.2% on Pro, and 1.2% on Enterprise. The company says the fee is passed to the customer, but buyers should confirm exactly how that appears on invoices during the demo.</p>

<p>Jobber charges 2.9% + 30c for online credit card payments and 2.7% + 30c for mobile in-field payments. Bank transfer (ACH) is 1% in the US. Jobber also offers instant payout for an additional 1% fee.</p>

### What the pricing does not tell you

<p>FieldFuze Core at $49/mo is bare-bones. It covers customer CRM, estimates, invoices, payment processing, and basic photo uploads with 3 seats. It does not include scheduling, inventory, time tracking, crew management, contracts, QuickBooks, or the other features that make FieldFuze stand out. Most serious buyers are really evaluating Pro at $349/mo.</p>

<p>Jobber Core at $29/mo annual is a solo-operator entry point. Many real teams will evaluate Connect or Grow quickly because client hub, automated reminders, QuickBooks sync, and job costing require higher tiers. Do not shop from the starting price alone. Map the features your team needs, then price the tier that includes them.</p>

## Where FieldFuze wins

### Seat-inclusive pricing

<p>The biggest differentiator is not a feature. It is the way FieldFuze Pro bundles 15 users into a flat $349/mo price. For a growing crew, that eliminates the per-user cost calculation that makes Jobber more expensive as headcount grows. Additional seats are $8.99 each, compared to Jobber's $29/user.</p>

### Inventory management

<p>FieldFuze includes inventory management on Pro and Enterprise. That matters for fence contractors, roofers, HVAC companies, plumbers, electricians, and landscapers that need to track materials assigned to jobs instead of managing parts in a spreadsheet. Jobber does not have native inventory management, which is one of the clearest reasons a growing crew would look past it. For a wider category scan, see the guide to <a href="/best-construction-inventory-management-software/">construction inventory management software</a>.</p>

### Contracts, e-signatures, and change orders

<p>FieldFuze Pro bundles contract management, e-signatures, change orders, recurring jobs, PDF merging, document management, and audit logs. That is important for contractors doing larger jobs than a simple service visit. Jobber handles quotes, approvals, invoices, forms, and customer communication well, but it is not trying to be the same contract and job-file workflow system.</p>

### No setup fees or contracts

<p>FieldFuze advertises no setup fees, no annual contracts, and being live in under 24 hours. Jobber also allows month-to-month billing, but FieldFuze's commitment profile is deliberately lighter than enterprise FSM tools that require multi-month rollouts.</p>

## Where Jobber wins

### Free trial and evaluation risk

<p>The 14-day no-card trial is the single biggest practical advantage. A contractor can test scheduling, quoting, client hub, payments, reminders, QuickBooks sync, job costing, mobile forms, and offline behavior with real jobs before spending money. FieldFuze requires a demo, which is appropriate for a crew-level product but heavier for a shop still validating its needs.</p>

### Independent review footprint

<p>Jobber has roughly 1,440 Capterra reviews and over 500 G2 reviews. The praise centers on scheduling, quoting, invoicing, customer communication, and the mobile app. The complaints flag QuickBooks sync friction, reporting limits, customization limits, and tier jumps. That public track record gives buyers confidence and specific diligence questions to ask. FieldFuze has a much thinner independent review footprint, which changes the diligence process.</p>

### Mobile app polish

<p>Jobber's iOS and Android apps are consistently praised. Techs can view job details, clock in and out, upload photos, add notes, fill out forms, and collect payment. Offline support is limited to timers, forms, notes, and attachments, with syncing after reconnection. FieldFuze has native mobile apps, but the public review volume is not deep enough to treat the mobile experience as proven at the same level. For the broader mobile-app shortlist, see the <a href="/field-service-management-mobile-app/">field service management mobile app</a> roundup.</p>

### Client Hub and customer communication

<p>Jobber's Client Hub gives customers a self-serve portal to request work, approve quotes, review appointments, see assigned team details, and pay invoices. That cuts down on "can you resend that invoice?" and "when are you coming?" calls. Automated "on my way" texts and appointment reminders are built around residential service work. FieldFuze has customer CRM and communication tools, but Jobber's client-facing workflow is more mature.</p>

### Pricing flexibility

<p>Jobber offers three billing modes: annual prepaid, monthly with no commitment, and monthly with a 1-year commitment. FieldFuze is month-to-month. Jobber's annual billing reduces cost for teams that can commit. The tradeoff is that annual billing is non-refundable if canceled early.</p>

## Feature-by-feature comparison

### Scheduling and dispatch

<p>Both platforms handle scheduling, but from different starting points. Jobber's drag-and-drop calendar is one of its strongest day-to-day tools and fits short-cycle service work. FieldFuze Pro includes jobs, scheduling, recurring jobs, crew management, and time tracking bundled with inventory and contract tools. If dispatch is your only problem, Jobber is the safer bet. If dispatch is tied to materials, contracts, and timecards, FieldFuze becomes more interesting.</p>

### Quoting and estimating

<p>Jobber's quote builder handles line items, optional add-ons, and digital approval with e-signature. Once approved, the quote converts to a job with one click. Follow-up automations can remind customers who have not responded. Jobber works well for residential service businesses that sell add-ons.</p>

<p>FieldFuze includes estimates and invoices on all plans. The difference is that FieldFuze pairs estimates with contract management, change orders, and document workflows on Pro, which matters for contractors doing larger jobs where the scope changes during the project.</p>

### Reporting and job costing

<p>Jobber publishes job costing on the Grow plan, including profit margin, labor, line items, and expenses for one-off and recurring jobs. It is functional for small service teams but not finance-grade. If you need deep technician efficiency trends or material cost variance reporting, expect to export data or pair Jobber with another tool.</p>

<p>FieldFuze includes time tracking, timecards, and an audit log on Pro. The operational reporting is built around crew management and job-linked costs rather than the financial reporting depth that enterprise tools provide. Neither platform replaces construction accounting.</p>

### QuickBooks integration

<p>FieldFuze offers two-way QuickBooks Online sync on Pro and Enterprise. Invoices, payments, and customer data sync between FieldFuze and QuickBooks. Jobber offers QuickBooks Online integration on Connect and above. The current sync runs from Jobber to QuickBooks. Buyers should test the sync with their actual chart of accounts, tax setup, and invoice workflow before going live with either platform.</p>

### Integrations and add-ons

<p>Jobber integrates with QuickBooks Online, Xero, Mailchimp, Gusto, The Home Depot, and Zapier for over 7,000 app connections. Add-ons include Marketing Suite ($79/mo), Jobber AI Receptionist ($99/mo), and Pipeline ($49/mo). For contractors evaluating CRM workflows, see the comparison of <a href="/jobber-vs-jobnimbus/">Jobber vs JobNimbus</a> for a CRM-focused alternative.</p>

<p>FieldFuze integrates with QuickBooks, Stripe, and EagleView (on Enterprise). The integration ecosystem is narrower than Jobber's, which is one of the tradeoffs of choosing a smaller, newer platform.</p>

## When to switch from Jobber to FieldFuze

<p>The switch starts to make sense when Jobber's per-user pricing and feature limits are the business constraint, not merely an annoyance. Examples: the crew has grown past 5 users and the monthly cost keeps climbing, inventory management is tracked in a separate spreadsheet, contract changes and e-signatures are handled outside the system, or the team needs time tracking and crew management tools that Jobber Core and Connect do not include.</p>

<p>Headcount is the rough guide. A 3-person team should probably stay on Jobber. A 10-person crew that needs inventory and contracts should evaluate FieldFuze Pro seriously. The better trigger is not just user count but whether the operational workflow has outgrown what Jobber bundles at the team tiers.</p>

## FAQ

### Is FieldFuze cheaper than Jobber?

<p>It depends on headcount. For a solo operator, Jobber Core at $29/mo annual is cheaper than FieldFuze Core at $49/mo. For a 10-person crew, FieldFuze Pro at $349/mo flat beats Jobber Grow team at $299/mo annual, but FieldFuze includes inventory, contracts, and other tools Jobber does not natively bundle. For a 15-person crew, FieldFuze Pro at $349/mo compares against Jobber Plus at $529/mo annual, a $180/mo difference. Run the math at your actual headcount, not the lowest advertised price.</p>

### Does FieldFuze have a free trial?

<p>No. FieldFuze does not advertise a self-serve free trial. The evaluation path is a demo or sales conversation. Jobber offers a 14-day free trial with no credit card required, which makes it easier to test if you want hands-on access before talking to a vendor.</p>

### Can FieldFuze replace Jobber for a growing team?

<p>Yes, if the team needs inventory management, contract tools, time tracking, and included seats. FieldFuze Pro bundles those at $349/mo with 15 seats. The tradeoff is the thinner independent review footprint and the demo-only evaluation path. Buyers should ask for customer references and run a real demo script before switching operations.</p>

### Which tool has better QuickBooks integration?

<p>Both integrate with QuickBooks Online. FieldFuze offers two-way sync on Pro and Enterprise. Jobber offers integration on Connect and above, with the current sync running from Jobber to QuickBooks. Test the sync with your actual chart of accounts, tax setup, and invoice workflow before going live with either platform.</p>

### Does FieldFuze include inventory management?

<p>Yes. Inventory management is included on Pro and Enterprise. That is one of the main reasons FieldFuze compares well against Jobber for crews that track parts, materials, and job-linked stock. Jobber does not have native inventory management.</p>

### Which tool is better for fence and deck contractors?

<p>FieldFuze has a dedicated fence page and covers line-item estimates, material tracking, multi-install scheduling, photos, and job-site invoicing. Jobber is a general field service platform that handles fence and deck work through quotes, scheduling, and invoices without trade-specific material templates. If material tracking per job is important, FieldFuze has the edge. If the priority is a free trial and a proven mobile app, Jobber is the safer starting point.</p>

### What is the biggest risk with FieldFuze?

<p>The biggest risk is evidence depth. FieldFuze publishes clear pricing and feature data, but it does not have the same independent review footprint as Jobber, Housecall Pro, or ServiceTitan. Buyers should use the demo to test real workflows with their own jobs, users, and QuickBooks setup before committing.</p>

### Should I try Jobber first and switch to FieldFuze later?

<p>Yes, that is a reasonable path for a solo operator or small team. Start with Jobber's 14-day trial. If the crew grows past 5 users and you find yourself needing inventory, contracts, or time tracking that Jobber does not natively include, evaluate FieldFuze Pro. The switch is a project, so document your workflows and data before migrating.</p>
