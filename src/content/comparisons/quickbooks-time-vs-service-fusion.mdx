# QuickBooks Time vs Service Fusion for Contractors

QuickBooks Time and Service Fusion are easy to compare on a search results page, but they solve different problems. QuickBooks Time is a time tracking layer for companies already working inside <a href="/quickbooks-online-review/">QuickBooks Online</a>. Service Fusion is a field service management system for companies that need customer records, estimates, jobs, scheduling, dispatch, invoicing, payments, and QuickBooks integration.

That difference matters more than the feature checklist. If your office only needs cleaner timesheets and payroll sync, Service Fusion is too much software. If your day breaks down at dispatch, job handoff, invoicing, customer records, and technician visibility, QuickBooks Time is not enough software.

## The real decision

### QuickBooks Time is a QuickBooks add-on decision

QuickBooks Time makes the most sense when QuickBooks Online is already the accounting backbone. The official pricing page says a QuickBooks Online account is required to use QuickBooks Time. That one line changes the buying math.

The standard Time Premium plan is $20 per month plus $8 per user per month. Time Elite is $40 per month plus $10 per user per month. QuickBooks currently advertises promotional base pricing, but evergreen buying math should use the standard rates because the promotion is temporary.

Add the required QuickBooks Online subscription before comparing totals. Using QuickBooks Online Simple Start at $38 per month, a 5-user QuickBooks Time Premium setup is $90 per month: $20 base, four additional users at $8, and $38 for QBO. A 10-user Premium setup is $130 per month. Elite is $118 per month for 5 users and $168 per month for 10 users with the same minimum QBO plan.

That's not a bad value if the company already uses QuickBooks Online. If QBO is already on the books, QuickBooks Time adds time tracking, payroll and invoicing flow, schedules, time off, alerts, photo attachments, and Time Kiosk without adding a separate operating system.

It's a weaker fit if you're not already a QuickBooks company. In that case, you're buying accounting software just to unlock the time tracker. That's usually backwards.

### Service Fusion is an operating-system decision

Service Fusion starts from the service day, not the timesheet. The official pricing page lists Starter, Plus, and Pro plans around customer management, estimates and jobs, scheduling and dispatching, QuickBooks integration, invoicing, payment processing, project management, reporting, and text messaging alerts.

Atlas now verifies Service Fusion as custom quote / demo-required. The visible pricing page shows Starter, Plus, and Pro packages, but it no longer shows public dollar amounts to a visitor. Buyers are directed to "Get Demo and Pricing" / "Talk to Sales," so current pricing should be confirmed in writing.

That access model can still change the math for a larger crew, but it requires a quote. A 10-user QuickBooks Time Premium setup with the minimum QBO subscription is $130 per month, so Service Fusion needs to be compared against a written quote rather than an old public price. QuickBooks Time is only solving time tracking. Service Fusion is covering dispatch, customer records, estimates, jobs, invoices, payments, and field workflow.

The better question is whether the company needs field service software at all. If yes, Service Fusion belongs in the comparison set with <a href="/jobber-vs-service-fusion/">Jobber vs Service Fusion</a>, <a href="/housecall-pro-vs-service-fusion/">Housecall Pro vs Service Fusion</a>, and <a href="/service-fusion-vs-workiz/">Service Fusion vs Workiz</a>. The broader shortlist should also include the full <a href="/jobber-review/">Jobber review</a>, <a href="/housecall-pro-review/">Housecall Pro review</a>, and <a href="/workiz-review/">Workiz review</a>. If no, QuickBooks Time may be the cleaner tool.

## Pricing reality

### Small QuickBooks shop

For a solo operator or small office that already pays for QuickBooks Online, QuickBooks Time is the lower-friction choice. The extra subscription is focused. You are paying for hours, schedules, time-off records, project time, and payroll or invoice flow inside the system you already use.

If the business has 3 to 5 people and the real problem is late timesheets, QuickBooks Time Premium is usually easier to justify than Service Fusion. A 5-user Premium setup at $90 per month with QBO Simple Start gives a visible planning number, while Service Fusion now requires a quote before the buyer can compare current dollar pricing.

That doesn't mean QuickBooks Time is a field service system. It means the company may not need a field service system yet.

### Growing service company

Once the office is coordinating multiple technicians, customer records, estimates, jobs, payment collection, invoice handoff, and daily dispatch, Service Fusion becomes more relevant. The buyer should ask for a written quote that covers the package, user terms, add-ons, onboarding, and billing term.

Service Fusion's visible pricing page still describes Starter, Plus, and Pro packages. Plus adds inventory management, job costing, job photo uploads, integrated voice and text, and additional review features. Pro adds custom documents, eSign documents, customer web portal, progress billing, and recurring invoicing.

Those aren't timesheet features. They are field service operating features. A 12-person company that needs dispatch, job records, invoices, and QuickBooks sync should compare Service Fusion against other FSM platforms, not against a time clock. For larger service companies, include the <a href="/servicetitan-review/">ServiceTitan review</a> as the enterprise benchmark before treating QuickBooks Time as the fallback.

### The crossover point is not just headcount

A pure headcount comparison can be misleading. QuickBooks Time Premium plus minimum QBO is $130 per month for 10 users. Service Fusion requires a quote, so the exact cost comparison depends on the package, user terms, add-ons, and billing term. On visible price alone, QuickBooks Time is easier to budget.

But if those 10 users need a dispatch board, customer history, job notes, work order flow, estimates, invoicing, payment processing, and mobile job details, the cheaper tool isn't solving the whole problem. If those 10 users only need hours sent to payroll, Service Fusion is overkill.

That's the buying split: timesheets versus operations.

## Where QuickBooks Time wins

### Native QuickBooks workflow

QuickBooks Time wins when the value is native QuickBooks flow. Time can support payroll, invoicing, and project tracking without the office exporting from a separate field service system and cleaning up records by hand.

That's especially useful for companies that already trust QuickBooks Online as the source of truth. The accounting system stays central. QuickBooks Time fills the labor-hour gap.

### Lower adoption burden

A time tracking rollout is still work, but it is smaller than a field service management rollout. The office needs users, permissions, jobs or projects, pay rules, time-off settings, schedules, and mobile expectations. That is manageable for a small team.

Service Fusion requires a deeper operational change: customer records, estimates, jobs, technician workflows, dispatch process, invoice flow, payment processing, QuickBooks sync, and plan-specific features. If the company is not ready to move the service day into software, that rollout can fail even if the product is capable.

### Time Kiosk and basic workforce controls

QuickBooks Time includes Time Kiosk on the plan cards. That matters for companies where workers punch in from a shared tablet at a shop, warehouse, or office. It also includes scheduling, time-off management, alerts, notifications, photo attachments, and support. Elite adds mileage tracking, project tracking, project estimates versus actuals, project activity feed, and timesheet signatures.

If those features cover the actual problem, don't buy a heavier platform just because it has more modules.

## Where QuickBooks Time falls short

### It requires QuickBooks Online

The required QBO subscription is the first limitation. If the company is not already on QuickBooks Online, QuickBooks Time forces an accounting decision before a time tracking decision. That adds cost and commitment.

For a contractor already using another accounting platform, payroll system, or field service tool, that dependency can be a deal-breaker.

### It is not a dispatch system

QuickBooks Time can help schedule and track time, but it is not designed to replace field service management software. It does not give the same customer, estimate, job, dispatch, invoice, payment, inventory, and customer portal workflow that a product like Service Fusion is built to provide.

If dispatchers are juggling job status, technician assignments, customer notes, invoices, and payments across several systems, QuickBooks Time will not fix that by itself. In that case, start with the <a href="/best-field-service-software/">field service software guide</a> and compare platforms built around the service day.

### Project depth sits on Elite

The features that matter most to field and project-oriented contractors sit higher in QuickBooks Time. Elite adds mileage tracking, project tracking, estimates versus actuals, project activity feed, and timesheet signatures. If those are the reason for the purchase, compare Elite totals, not Premium totals.

## Where Service Fusion wins

### Full field service workflow

Service Fusion wins when the company needs a field service operating system. Starter includes customer management, estimates and jobs, scheduling and dispatching, QuickBooks integration, invoicing and payment processing, project management and reporting, text alerts, estimate options, and company reviews.

That is the workday for a service contractor. The call comes in, the estimate is built, the job is scheduled, the tech is dispatched, payment is collected, and the record moves back toward accounting.

### Broad team access

Broad team access is one of the most important Service Fusion buying questions. A five-person team may not care. A 20-person team cares a lot.

With Service Fusion, the question should be package level, access terms, and total quote rather than a simple per-seat math problem. That can make budgeting easier for companies with technicians, dispatchers, office staff, managers, and owners who all need access, but only if the quote confirms those users are included.

### QuickBooks Online and Desktop integration

Service Fusion's official FAQ says it integrates with both QuickBooks Online and Desktop versions. That matters because many contractors still run QuickBooks Desktop or have a bookkeeper tied to a Desktop workflow.

QuickBooks Time is naturally tied to QuickBooks Online. Service Fusion is a better fit when QuickBooks is important, but the company does not want QuickBooks Online to be the required center of the software stack.

### Higher-tier job management features

Plus and Pro add the features that make Service Fusion more than Starter dispatch software. Plus adds inventory management and job costing. Pro adds customer web portal, custom documents, eSign documents, progress billing, and recurring invoicing.

Service Fusion also lists add-ons such as ServiceCall.ai and GPS Fleet Tracking. Treat those as separate evaluation items. Ask for the add-on cost sheet before comparing total cost against another field service platform.

## Where Service Fusion falls short

### It is too much if timesheets are the only problem

Do not buy Service Fusion just because timesheets are messy. A field service rollout is heavier than a time tracking rollout. If the business already has customers, schedules, estimates, invoices, and payment workflow under control, Service Fusion may duplicate systems the office does not want to replace.

A small QuickBooks shop that only needs hours to reach payroll should start with QuickBooks Time or another time tracking tool before buying a full FSM platform.

### Pricing is demo-led

Service Fusion's current visible pricing page is demo-led. The page shows packages, but it does not show public dollar amounts. That can still be a good fit for larger teams, but it is a meaningful buying difference from QuickBooks Time, where a small crew can calculate a budget before talking to sales.

The billing detail matters. Ask whether annual billing is required, whether month-to-month is available, and what happens if the workflow does not fit. If the company is not certain, the demo and pilot need to be serious.

### Mobile fit should be tested

Service Fusion's value depends on technicians actually using the field workflow. Before committing, test the mobile experience with the devices your crew carries. Build a real day: schedule a job, dispatch it, add notes and photos, capture payment, update status, and sync back to the office.

This is especially important if the team is Android-heavy or often works in low-connectivity areas. A field service platform only works if the field team uses it.

## Evaluation plan

### Test QuickBooks Time around payroll week

Do not test QuickBooks Time with fake punches. Test it through the week that currently hurts.

Set up the real users, jobs or projects, schedules, time-off rules, and approval flow. Have workers clock in and out from their actual devices. Run payroll prep. Check whether the office still has to chase missing hours, correct job codes, or retype data into QuickBooks.

The pass-fail question is simple: did QuickBooks Time reduce payroll cleanup without creating a heavier admin burden?

### Test Service Fusion around a dispatch day

For Service Fusion, the test should be a real service day. Build customers, estimates, jobs, technician assignments, dispatch board changes, payment collection, invoice flow, and QuickBooks sync. If inventory, job costing, customer portal, progress billing, or recurring invoicing are buying reasons, test the tier where those features live.

The pass-fail question is different: did Service Fusion give the office a clearer way to run jobs from request to payment?

### Compare against the right alternatives

If QuickBooks Time feels close but too QuickBooks-dependent, compare it against <a href="/quickbooks-time-vs-clockshark/">ClockShark</a>, <a href="/quickbooks-time-vs-workiz/">Workiz</a>, and the <a href="/best-time-tracking-software-contractors/">best contractor time tracking software</a> roundup.

If Service Fusion feels close but heavy, compare it against <a href="/jobber-vs-service-fusion/">Jobber</a>, <a href="/housecall-pro-vs-service-fusion/">Housecall Pro</a>, and <a href="/service-fusion-vs-workiz/">Workiz</a>. Those are more natural alternatives because they compete for the full field service workflow.

## Final verdict

Choose QuickBooks Time if your business already runs on QuickBooks Online and the problem is time. It is a focused add-on for labor hours, schedules, time off, kiosk punches, payroll flow, invoicing flow, and project time. The required QBO subscription makes the math worse for non-QuickBooks shops, but for companies already paying for QuickBooks Online, the adoption path is straightforward.

Choose Service Fusion if your business needs a field service system. It covers a broader workflow: customers, estimates, jobs, scheduling, dispatch, invoicing, payments, QuickBooks integration, and broad team access. It is not the right purchase for a company that only needs timesheets. It is the better fit when the day breaks between the office, dispatcher, technician, customer, and bookkeeper, but the quote needs to prove the economics.

The cleanest rule: buy QuickBooks Time when the missing record is the timecard. Buy Service Fusion when the missing record is the job.
