# QuickBooks Time vs Workiz: which contractor tool fits?

QuickBooks Time and Workiz can both touch the field crew, but they solve different problems. QuickBooks Time is a time tracking product for companies already using, or willing to use, QuickBooks Online. It helps the office collect time, approve timesheets, track mileage, see who is working, and move labor data into the QuickBooks ecosystem.

Workiz is a broader field service platform. It is built around jobs, customers, calls, dispatching, routing, estimates, payments, and technician communication. Time and technician visibility matter inside Workiz, but the buying reason is usually office control: fewer lost calls, cleaner dispatch handoffs, and one place for the customer and job record.

A contractor choosing between them should start by naming the bottleneck: payroll-ready time data or call-to-dispatch control.</p>

## The buying split

### QuickBooks Time is the narrower time tracking choice

QuickBooks Time makes sense when the job is specific: collect accurate time from employees, approve it, attach it to customers or projects, and send it toward payroll or job reporting. The official QuickBooks Time pricing page says a QuickBooks Online account is required. It lists Time Premium at $20/month base plus $8/user/month and Time Elite at $40/month base plus $10/user/month, with temporary promotional pricing shown separately.

That matters because the headline time-tracking price is not the full buying decision for a non-QuickBooks shop. If the company does not already use QuickBooks Online, it is also buying into that accounting platform. For contractors already deep in QuickBooks, that requirement is not a penalty. It is the reason to choose QuickBooks Time in the first place.

The product fit is straightforward. QuickBooks Time is strongest when the office needs simple timesheets, mobile clock-in and clock-out, manager approvals, location context, mileage, and project tracking tied to accounting. It is not trying to replace a full field service dispatch board.

### Workiz is the broader field service choice

Workiz should be judged against a different day. If calls keep coming in, jobs move during the day, techs need updated notes, customers need texts, and payments need to be collected in the field, Workiz is closer to the operating problem. Its official pricing page presents Standard, Pro, and Ultimate as request-pricing plans and shows a feature matrix that includes scheduling, jobs, estimates, payments, QuickBooks Online sync, automations, and Workiz Communication as a separately sold add-on.

The pricing caution is real. Workiz publishes extra-member annual-payment rates: Standard extra members at $55/month and Pro extra members at $65/month. It also says card readers are sold separately and subscription prices exclude applicable sales tax. The buyer should not compare QuickBooks Time's public base fee against an incomplete Workiz number. Get the base plan, extra members, phone/SMS, AI answering, communication tools, card readers, taxes, and onboarding assumptions in writing.

That quote-led model is worth dealing with only when Workiz is solving more than time tracking. If the company only needs a digital time clock, Workiz is probably too much system. If the office is losing revenue to missed calls, dispatch confusion, weak technician visibility, or scattered job communication, Workiz deserves a closer look.

### If the company already pays for QuickBooks Online

QuickBooks Time becomes easier to justify. The accounting platform is already in place, so the time tracking decision is mostly about whether the field team will use the mobile app, whether managers will approve time inside the workflow, and whether the payroll or job-costing handoff saves enough office work.

For that buyer, Workiz is not a cheaper substitute. It is a different operating system. It may still be the better choice if dispatch and customer communication are the bigger pain, but the company should not buy it merely because it also has technician and job visibility.

### If the company does not use QuickBooks Online

The QuickBooks Time math gets harder. The official pricing page requires QuickBooks Online, so the buyer has to price the accounting subscription along with Time Premium or Time Elite. That can still be rational if the company also wants to move accounting into QuickBooks. It is a poor fit if the business only wants standalone crew time tracking.

In that case, compare QuickBooks Time against standalone time tracking tools as well as Workiz. If the real need is GPS time tracking, geofencing, payroll export, and job labor reporting, a field crew time tracker may be cleaner than buying QuickBooks Online plus QuickBooks Time. If the real need is dispatch operations, compare Workiz against Jobber, Housecall Pro, Service Fusion, and ServiceTitan.

## Where QuickBooks Time wins

### Native QuickBooks workflow

QuickBooks Time's strongest advantage is not a feature list. It is the accounting fit. For a contractor already using QuickBooks Online, time can move toward payroll, invoicing, and project reporting with less export work. That matters for owners who trust QuickBooks as the financial source of truth and want fewer disconnected systems.

Workiz lists QuickBooks Online sync on its pricing page, but that is not the same as owning the whole QuickBooks workflow. If accounting is the center of the buying decision, QuickBooks Time gets the first test.

### Published time tracking rates

QuickBooks Time publishes the core time tracking rates clearly: Premium at $20/month base plus $8/user/month and Elite at $40/month base plus $10/user/month. The official page may show temporary promotional pricing, but evergreen buying math should use the standard base and per-user rates.

That clarity helps small teams budget the time tracking layer. The buyer still needs to account for QuickBooks Online, but the Time plan itself is easier to model than Workiz's quote-led base plans.

### Simple fit for time, mileage, and approvals

If the problem is late timesheets and payroll prep, QuickBooks Time stays focused. The official feature page emphasizes simple timesheets, mobile time tracking, who is working, customizable reports, integrations, geofencing, and mileage/project tools on eligible plans. That is the right scope for a company that does not need to rebuild dispatch.

## Where Workiz wins

### Dispatch and communication depth

Workiz wins when the office needs more than a clock. Its pricing and feature pages put scheduling, jobs, estimates, online payments, QuickBooks Online sync, automations, phone, SMS, call insights, AI answering, call masking, ad tracking, and communication tools in the same buying conversation. That is a stronger fit for service businesses where the first customer touch is a phone call and the job may move several times before it closes.

QuickBooks Time can show who is working and help track time by job, but it does not replace the dispatch process. Workiz is more relevant when the office wants call history, messages, customer records, estimates, dispatch decisions, and payments attached to the same job flow.

### Better fit for reactive trades

Workiz is especially worth comparing for locksmiths, garage door companies, appliance repair, junk removal, HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and similar trades where calls and routes change all day. The value is not just that a technician can be assigned to a job. It is that the office can see the customer, estimate, job, route, messages, payment, and follow-up context together.

QuickBooks Time can still work for those teams if the only pain is timesheets. But when the business is losing revenue between the call and the dispatched job, a time tracking product is too narrow.

### Quote-led, but more complete

Workiz's quote-led pricing is a drawback for quick budgeting. It is also a sign that the buyer needs to price a fuller system. The official page separates Workiz Communication and AI Answering, flags card readers and sales tax, and publishes extra-member rates for Standard and Pro. That is more work than reading a simple time tracking price table, but it gives the buyer a checklist for the actual operating cost.

## When each tool is the wrong choice

<p><strong>Do not choose QuickBooks Time</strong> if you are trying to fix dispatch, call handling, customer communication, estimate follow-up, or payment collection. It may improve timesheets, but it will not become the service business command center.</p>

<p><strong>Do not choose Workiz</strong> if the company only needs time tracking for payroll. A quote-led field service platform is hard to justify when the real requirement is clock-in, clock-out, approvals, and payroll sync.</p>

<p><strong>Be careful with both</strong> if you are a one-person operation. QuickBooks Time makes most sense when QuickBooks Online is already justified. Workiz makes most sense when dispatch and communication problems are already costing money. If neither condition is true, start with a lighter tool.</p>

## Evaluation plan

### Test QuickBooks Time against payroll week

Do not evaluate QuickBooks Time by clicking around a demo account for an hour. Test the payroll week. Create a real customer or project, have employees clock in from mobile, edit a timesheet, approve time, check GPS or location expectations, test mileage if it matters, and walk the data toward payroll or invoicing. If the QuickBooks handoff saves office time, the product has a reason to stay.

Also test the plan gate. If geofencing, mileage, project activity, or estimates versus actuals are the reason for buying, confirm whether Premium or Elite is the real tier. A contractor who buys the cheaper plan and misses the one needed feature will blame the software when the real problem was plan selection.

### Test Workiz against a dispatch-heavy day

Test Workiz with the day that causes trouble. Add a call or lead, create an estimate, schedule a job, move the technician, send a customer message, collect payment, and close the job. Then ask what the office can see at each step. If the workflow keeps calls, texts, notes, jobs, routes, and payments together, Workiz is solving a real field service problem.

Before signing, ask sales for the written all-in quote: base plan, included members, extra members, Workiz Communication, phone/SMS usage, AI answering, card readers, sales tax, onboarding, and any Ultimate-only features. If the quote only covers the base plan, it is not enough information.

## Final verdict

<p><strong>Our call:</strong> QuickBooks Time is the better choice for QuickBooks Online users who need time tracking. Workiz is the better choice for dispatch-heavy field service teams that need a broader operating platform.</p>

<p><strong>Choose QuickBooks Time if:</strong> accounting already runs through QuickBooks, the office needs payroll-ready timesheets, and the team can solve scheduling, dispatch, phones, estimates, and payments somewhere else.</p>

## FAQ

### Is QuickBooks Time or Workiz better for contractors?

QuickBooks Time is better for contractors already using QuickBooks Online who mainly need time tracking, approvals, mileage, and payroll-ready timesheets. Workiz is better for contractors who need full field service workflow around calls, dispatching, estimates, customer communication, payments, and technician routing.

### Does QuickBooks Time require QuickBooks Online?

Yes. The official QuickBooks Time pricing page says a QuickBooks Online account is required to use QuickBooks Time. That means non-QuickBooks users should include the separate QuickBooks Online cost before comparing QuickBooks Time with Workiz or standalone time tracking tools.

### How much does QuickBooks Time cost?

Atlas-verified QuickBooks Time pricing lists Time Premium at $20/month base plus $8/user/month and Time Elite at $40/month base plus $10/user/month. The official page may show temporary promotional base discounts, but evergreen buyer math should use the standard rates and remember that QuickBooks Online is required.

### How much does Workiz cost?

Workiz uses request pricing for Standard, Pro, and Ultimate. Its official pricing page publishes extra-member annual-payment rates of $55/month for Standard extra members and $65/month for Pro extra members. Workiz Communication, phone/SMS, AI answering, card readers, taxes, and other add-ons should be confirmed in the written quote.

### Is Workiz just a time tracking alternative to QuickBooks Time?

No. Workiz is broader field service software. It can support technician visibility and job workflow, but its buying case is dispatch, calls, customer communication, estimates, payments, and routing. If all you need is time tracking, compare QuickBooks Time with standalone time clock tools before buying Workiz.

### Which tool is better for a small HVAC, plumbing, or locksmith company?

If the company already uses QuickBooks Online and payroll prep is the main issue, QuickBooks Time is the cleaner first test. If the office is losing jobs through missed calls, dispatch changes, scattered texts, and poor technician visibility, Workiz is the stronger fit for HVAC, plumbing, locksmith, garage door, appliance repair, and similar reactive service teams.
