# AutoCAD LT vs DraftSight for Contractors

AutoCAD LT and DraftSight solve the same practical problem for a contractor: creating, editing, and exchanging DWG drawings without paying for a full 3D design suite. Both can work for site plans, shop drawings, permit markups, as-built updates, and consultant file exchange. The difference is risk tolerance.

AutoCAD LT is the safer default when DWG compatibility is the job. Autodesk's official AutoCAD LT page positions it as 2D design and drafting software for designers, engineers, and construction professionals, with DWG drafting, 2D documentation, AutoLISP support, and desktop, web, and mobile access. Atlas verifies the pricing at $70/month, $540/year, or $1,620 for three years.

DraftSight is the lower-cost alternative. Dassault Systèmes publishes DraftSight Professional at $299/year, Premium at $599/year, and Network starting at $399/year. The official page says Professional can create, view, and edit DWG, DXF, and DGN files and includes design and automation tools, including AutoLISP. That makes it a real CAD option, not just a viewer. The question is whether it passes your actual file test.

## The fast answer

AutoCAD LT wins when a contractor needs the least risky DWG workflow. If your files move between architects, engineers, commercial GCs, municipalities, consultants, and internal project teams, the Autodesk-native choice removes a lot of explaining. Nobody has to ask whether the drawing will open correctly in AutoCAD because AutoCAD LT is part of the same ecosystem.

DraftSight wins when the cost gap matters and the workflow is mostly standard 2D drafting. At $299/year for Professional, DraftSight costs materially less than AutoCAD LT's $540/year annual plan. For a small shop that drafts regularly but does not need full Autodesk ecosystem depth, that money matters.

The shortcut: if a broken drawing exchange would delay a permit, bid, or consultant review, start with AutoCAD LT. If the work is mostly internal drafting and your team can test files carefully before committing, DraftSight deserves the trial.

## Pricing and buying motion

The pricing comparison is simple on the surface.

AutoCAD LT is verified in Atlas as a commercial single-user subscription: $70 paid monthly, $540/year when paid annually, or $1,620 over three years. Autodesk's official overview page shows AutoCAD LT starting at $45/month, which aligns with the annual plan's monthly equivalent. The same official source points users to a free trial path, and Atlas records the trial as 15 days.

DraftSight publishes lower annual entry pricing. The official DraftSight buying page lists Professional at $299 USD/year, Premium at $599 USD/year, and Network starting at $399 USD/year. The same page mentions a promotional anniversary discount in cart, but CSH recommendations should use standard rates as the budget baseline because promotions can expire or vary.

For a contractor, the important math is not only first-year price. AutoCAD LT can be bought monthly for seasonal drafting needs. DraftSight Professional is annual. If a contractor needs CAD for one short permit package, AutoCAD LT's monthly path may be cheaper. If the business needs a year-round seat, DraftSight's $299/year is the lower published commitment. If perpetual-license economics are the real reason you are comparing CAD tools, add [ProgeCAD](/progecad-review/) to the shortlist before deciding.

## DWG compatibility and file-exchange risk

This is the core decision.

AutoCAD LT is not just DWG-compatible. It is the Autodesk 2D drafting product built around DWG. That matters when files leave your office. Commercial construction workflows depend on clean layers, xrefs, plot styles, fonts, line weights, title blocks, dimensions, and PDF output. A drawing that technically opens but plots wrong can still waste a day.

DraftSight's official product page says Professional opens, edits, and saves DWG, DXF, and DGN files. That is enough for many 2D drafting workflows. It also supports PDF import, Draw Compare, parametric blocks, and AutoLISP/Visual LISP automation. The product is credible.

The risk is not whether DraftSight exists as a serious CAD tool. It does. The risk is whether it reproduces your specific workflow under deadline pressure. A contractor comparing the two should use the DraftSight trial to open real past project files, bind and reload xrefs, print with the office's CTB or STB styles, export permit PDFs, check SHX fonts, test parametric blocks, and confirm that consultant files round-trip cleanly.

If that test passes, DraftSight's price advantage becomes real. If it fails, AutoCAD LT's higher annual price is cheaper than workarounds.

## 2D drafting, automation, and daily use

Both products cover the practical 2D CAD tasks contractors usually need: linework, dimensions, layers, blocks, annotation, PDF import/export, and drawing comparison. Neither should be confused with a full construction project management system or estimating platform. This is drawing software.

AutoCAD LT's official overview highlights 2D drafting and documentation, DWG creation, Geometry Cleanup, Smart Blocks, and AutoLISP. For contractors, AutoLISP matters because it can enforce CAD standards and automate repetitive work. Layer cleanup, title block checks, dimension routines, and batch tasks can save meaningful time on multi-sheet drawing sets.

DraftSight also supports AutoLISP and Visual LISP, according to the official product source. That is one of its stronger arguments for AutoCAD-trained users. If a shop already has small LISP routines for drafting cleanup or standards, DraftSight may preserve enough of that workflow to make switching realistic.

The daily-use difference is ecosystem depth. AutoCAD LT benefits from Autodesk documentation, training, hiring familiarity, and the assumption that outside parties know the product. DraftSight benefits from lower cost, an AutoCAD-familiar interface, DGN support, and Dassault's connection to SOLIDWORKS workflows. One is safer by default. The other asks for a more deliberate rollout.

## 3D, BIM, and when neither tool is enough

AutoCAD LT is intentionally 2D. Autodesk's official page points users to full AutoCAD when they need 3D modeling, rendering, and additional documentation toolsets. That means AutoCAD LT is the wrong buy if your company needs client-facing 3D visuals, model-based design, or specialized architecture, electrical, mechanical, MEP, Plant 3D, Map 3D, or Raster Design toolsets.

DraftSight Professional is also primarily a 2D drafting decision. DraftSight Premium adds 3D capabilities, custom blocks, STEP import, Sheet Set Manager, DGN export, and BIM module connections according to the official buying page. That gives DraftSight a broader path above Professional, but it does not automatically make Premium a substitute for full AutoCAD, Revit, SOLIDWORKS, SketchUp, or Chief Architect.

For contractors, the buying question should stay grounded: are you producing 2D drawings and markups, or are you designing in 3D? If the answer is 2D DWG work, AutoCAD LT and DraftSight belong on the shortlist. If the answer is residential design presentations, compare [Chief Architect](/chief-architect-review/) and [SketchUp](/sketchup-review/). If the answer is plan markup or takeoff from PDFs instead of CAD drafting, compare [Bluebeam Revu](/bluebeam-revu-review/). For a wider category view, use the [CAD software for contractors](/best-cad-software-contractors/) guide before forcing either tool into the job.

## Where AutoCAD LT wins

**Lower file risk.** AutoCAD LT is the safer choice when DWG exchange has external consequences. If a GC, architect, engineer, or municipality expects AutoCAD-native files, AutoCAD LT keeps the conversation simple.

**Better ecosystem depth.** Training, documentation, hiring familiarity, support resources, and consultant expectations all favor Autodesk. That does not mean every contractor needs it, but it matters when CAD is a business-critical workflow.

**Flexible monthly access.** Atlas verifies AutoCAD LT at $70/month and $540/year. For seasonal or occasional drafting, the monthly option can beat an annual seat if you only need CAD during specific project windows.

**Clean 2D scope.** AutoCAD LT is easy to position internally: it is 2D drafting and documentation. If the company needs more, upgrade the tool. If it does not, LT avoids paying full AutoCAD pricing for unused 3D features.

## Where DraftSight wins

**Lower annual entry cost.** DraftSight Professional at $299/year is the obvious advantage. The savings are meaningful for small teams, especially when several seats are needed.

**Broad 2D file support.** DraftSight's official pages cite DWG, DXF, and DGN file support. DGN matters for contractors that see infrastructure, civil, or owner-provided drawings from MicroStation-adjacent environments.

**AutoCAD-familiar workflow.** Contractors with AutoCAD-trained users do not have to learn a completely foreign drafting model. Commands, drafting concepts, and LISP-based habits can carry over better than they would in a simpler sketching tool.

**Network licensing path.** DraftSight's official buying page lists network licensing starting at $399/year. For firms that need simultaneous usage and license deployment controls, that option may matter more than the named-user price.

## Where each product is the wrong choice

AutoCAD LT is the wrong choice when the company does not actually need CAD every month. If you only need a few simple sketches per year, a local draftsperson, plan service, or lighter tool may be cheaper than carrying a subscription.

AutoCAD LT is also the wrong choice when 3D is the real requirement. It does not include 3D modeling. If client visuals, model coordination, or specialized toolsets are central to the job, full AutoCAD, Revit, SketchUp, Chief Architect, or another category-specific product belongs in the discussion.

DraftSight is the wrong choice when compatibility must be assumed instead of tested. If your team will not run a real trial with past project files, do not treat the lower price as free money. The savings only matter after the file workflow proves itself.

DraftSight is also the wrong choice if your team depends on Autodesk-specific plugins, deep third-party training, or consultants who only support AutoCAD workflows. A smaller ecosystem is not a flaw for every buyer, but it is a real cost when deadlines are tight.

## Evaluation plan

For AutoCAD LT, test the workflow you actually need to run. Open a real architect DWG, apply markups, check layers, import a PDF, export a permit PDF, and confirm that web or mobile access helps field review. If you already know AutoCAD, the trial should focus on whether LT's 2D-only scope is enough compared with full AutoCAD.

For DraftSight, make the trial stricter. Use the messiest real files you have: title blocks, xrefs, SHX fonts, CTB/STB plot styles, parametric blocks, consultant files, raster references, and PDF exports. Print to the actual plotter or PDF driver your team uses. Test any LISP routines before buying. Ask the person who will be blamed when a file breaks to sign off on the result.

Do not evaluate either tool from feature checklists alone. CAD purchases fail when the buyer checks "DWG support" but never tests the exact drawing standards, printers, consultants, and permit outputs the business relies on.

## Bottom line for contractors

AutoCAD LT is the better default when outside file exchange matters. It costs more than DraftSight Professional, but the premium buys the Autodesk-native path, a larger ecosystem, and less compatibility uncertainty. For commercial contractors, estimators, and project teams that work with architects, engineers, GCs, and municipalities, that safety is often worth the difference.

DraftSight is the smarter value play when the files pass testing. It gives contractors a real 2D CAD option at $299/year, with DWG/DXF/DGN support, AutoLISP/Visual LISP, and an AutoCAD-familiar workflow. The savings are not theoretical, but they have to be earned through a trial that uses real project files.

My recommendation: choose AutoCAD LT if file risk is expensive. Choose DraftSight if budget pressure is real, the drafting workflow is mostly 2D, and your compatibility test is clean. ## Frequently asked questions

**Is DraftSight cheaper than AutoCAD LT?**

Yes. Atlas verifies AutoCAD LT at $540/year on the annual plan, while DraftSight's official buying page lists Professional at $299/year. AutoCAD LT also has a $70/month option, which can be cheaper for short seasonal use. For a year-round seat, DraftSight Professional has the lower published entry price.

**Is DraftSight as compatible as AutoCAD LT?**

DraftSight supports DWG, DXF, and DGN files, and it is a credible CAD product. But compatibility should be tested with real project files before purchase. AutoCAD LT is the Autodesk-native option, so it carries less file-exchange risk when drawings move between architects, engineers, GCs, consultants, and permit offices.

**Does AutoCAD LT or DraftSight include 3D modeling?**

AutoCAD LT does not include 3D modeling. DraftSight Professional is primarily a 2D drafting tool, while DraftSight Premium adds 3D and BIM-related capabilities. Contractors that need serious 3D modeling or BIM coordination should compare full AutoCAD, Revit, SOLIDWORKS, SketchUp, Chief Architect, or other category-specific tools.

**Which tool is better for contractors?**

AutoCAD LT is better for contractors whose drawing files must work cleanly with outside teams. DraftSight is better for cost-sensitive contractors who need 2D DWG drafting and can validate compatibility during the trial. The deciding factor is not feature count. It is the cost of a file problem in your workflow.
