# Freshsales vs MailerLite for Contractor Follow-Up

Freshsales and MailerLite both help contractors follow up with leads and customers, but they are built for different jobs. Freshsales is a sales CRM. MailerLite is an email marketing platform.

That distinction matters more than it sounds. Freshsales is for the contractor that wants contact records, deal pipelines, lead scoring, built-in phone calling, and sales accountability in one system. MailerLite is for the contractor that wants newsletters, forms, landing pages, simple automations, and a clean way to keep a contact list warm. Both have real free plans, but they solve different problems.

## Quick verdict

Pick Freshsales if the contractor office needs a sales system. The official Freshsales pricing page positions the product as a CRM with contact management, deal pipelines, kanban views, built-in phone, email templates, AI lead scoring, and workflow automation. For a contractor, that points to lead intake, deal tracking, call accountability, and pipeline visibility - the work that happens before a quote becomes a job.

Pick MailerLite if the contractor office already has contacts but communication is inconsistent. The official MailerLite pricing and feature pages position the product around newsletters, email automations, landing pages, websites, forms, pop-ups, subscriber segmentation, reporting, and affordable plans. For a contractor, that points to campaigns: past-customer reactivation, seasonal reminders, quote follow-up emails, and simple lead capture.

Skip both if the problem is field operations. Neither product replaces [Jobber](/jobber-review/), [Housecall Pro](/housecall-pro-review/), [ServiceTitan](/servicetitan-review/), or a broader [field service software](/best-field-service-software/) platform. Freshsales and MailerLite can help before and around the sale. They do not dispatch crews, route technicians, track production, manage job costing, or run field workflows by themselves.

## When Freshsales makes more sense

Freshsales makes more sense when the follow-up problem is tied to sales process ownership. A lead comes in from a form. Someone needs to call. Someone needs to move the opportunity through a pipeline, track the deal stage, set a next action, and keep the customer record clean. That is broader than email marketing.

Freshsales's official pricing page lists a free plan for up to 3 users with contact management, kanban deals, email templates, built-in phone, and live chat. The paid tiers add more: Growth at $9 per user per month billed annually includes contact lifecycle stages, basic workflows, contextual collaboration, product catalog, and a CPQ license. Pro at $39 per user per month adds Freddy AI contact scoring, sales emails, deal insights, custom sales activities, territory management, sales sequences, multiple sales pipelines, and advanced workflows. Enterprise at $59 per user per month adds field-level permissions, custom modules, forecasting insights, and audit logs.

A roofing, remodeling, HVAC, or specialty trade contractor might use Freshsales to:

- capture a lead from a form or marketplace
- tag the lead by service line, source, or urgency
- call the lead directly from the CRM using built-in phone
- move the deal through pipeline stages: new, qualified, estimate scheduled, quote sent, won, lost
- use Freddy AI lead scoring to prioritize which leads to call first
- set follow-up tasks and next actions on each deal
- track quote status and deal value in one board

That is the Freshsales argument. The free plan is real, the phone is built in, and the pipeline is visual. The tradeoff is that Freshsales is a sales CRM - it does not manage job scheduling, dispatch, invoicing, production, or field workflows.

## When MailerLite makes more sense

MailerLite makes more sense when the contractor has a list problem or a campaign problem. The business may already have names and emails from old estimates, past customers, seasonal maintenance work, website forms, trade shows, financing inquiries, or referrals. The problem is that those contacts rarely receive useful follow-up.

MailerLite is built for that layer. Its official pages describe newsletters, email automations, landing pages, websites, forms, pop-ups, segmentation, reporting, A/B testing, and a drag-and-drop email editor. For a contractor, that maps to practical marketing work:

- send a quote follow-up sequence after an estimate request
- reactivate old estimates before a seasonal promotion
- remind past customers about maintenance, inspections, warranties, or upgrades
- create a landing page for one service or neighborhood
- collect new subscribers with forms and pop-ups
- segment contacts by service interest, location, source, or customer type
- review campaign reports before the next send

That does not make MailerLite a replacement for a CRM. It can store subscribers, tags, forms, and automation paths, but it is not the natural place to manage every open quote, owner, deal value, probability, estimator assignment, or won/lost reason. The best MailerLite buyer says: we need to communicate better with people we already know.

## Pricing comparison

Both products publish a free entry point and tiered paid plans, but the billing unit is different. Freshsales prices around CRM users and edition depth. MailerLite prices around subscriber count and email marketing capacity.

Monthly billing is Growth $11, Pro $47, and Enterprise $71 per user per month. A 21-day free trial with no credit card required is available on paid plans. Freshsales also offers optional add-ons for CPQ and Freddy AI Agent sessions, both priced separately from the core CRM plans.

Enterprise is custom for businesses with more than 200,000 subscribers. A 14-day premium-feature trial with no credit card required is available. Pricing scales as the subscriber list grows.

## CRM and sales pipeline depth

Freshsales wins the CRM comparison. It is built to manage contacts, accounts, deals, pipelines, activities, and workflows. The free plan includes contact management, kanban deal views, email templates, and built-in phone. Growth adds lifecycle stages, basic workflows, and a CPQ license. Pro adds Freddy AI contact scoring, deal insights, sales sequences, multiple sales pipelines, territory management, and custom sales activities.

For contractors, that matters when follow-up depends on status. A new roof replacement lead may need one response. A quoted-but-not-sold lead may need another. A customer who accepted a quote but has not paid may need a different message. A past customer may need a seasonal reminder. Freshsales is stronger when those actions should depend on the CRM record and sales stage.

MailerLite does not compete there directly. It can segment subscribers and trigger automations based on tags, form submissions, or subscriber behavior. But its record is a marketing contact, not a sales pipeline record. That is fine if another system owns sales accountability. It is a problem if the contractor expects MailerLite to become the office's lead, quote, and estimator command center.

Decision signal: choose Freshsales when the question is "who owns this lead and what stage is it in?" Choose MailerLite when the question is "which list or segment should receive this campaign?" For a deeper CRM comparison, see [Freshsales vs Pipeline CRM](/freshsales-vs-pipeline-crm/) or [Freshsales vs Zoho CRM](/freshsales-vs-zoho-crm/).

## Email marketing and automation

MailerLite wins the email marketing comparison. The product is designed around emails, landing pages, forms, automations, subscriber lists, reports, and audience growth. That makes it easier for a small office or marketing coordinator to start with a simple workflow.

A practical MailerLite setup could look like this:

1. A landing page captures storm-damage inspection leads.
2. A form tags the subscriber by service area.
3. A five-email sequence explains the inspection process.
4. A separate campaign goes to old estimates before peak season.
5. Reports show which subject lines, forms, and links performed.

Freshsales can send email, but its center of gravity is the CRM record. The free plan includes email templates. Growth adds basic workflows. Pro adds sales sequences and advanced workflows. But Freshsales is not built for newsletter design, A/B testing, subscriber growth tools, landing page builders, or campaign reporting. If the contractor just needs a clean email marketing tool, MailerLite will feel faster and more purpose-built.

If the contractor needs campaigns to trigger from pipeline movement, quote activity, or customer record updates, Freshsales is the stronger fit because the email action can sit closer to the deal.

## Built-in phone and AI features

Freshsales has a built-in phone system. The free plan includes 200 minutes per month, and Pro and above support Bring Your Own Carrier for more volume. That matters for contractors where the sales process still depends on calling leads. A contractor can call a lead, log the call, set a follow-up task, and update the deal stage without leaving the CRM.

Freshsales also includes Freddy AI on the Pro plan and above. Freddy AI provides contact scoring, deal insights, sales email writing, and text rephrase/expand features. That helps prioritize which leads to call first and speeds up email communication. The free plan does not include Freddy AI, but the Growth plan at $9 per user per month is the entry point for basic workflows. Pro at $39 per user per month unlocks the full AI feature set.

MailerLite has no built-in phone system. If phone follow-up is central to the sales process, the team needs a separate calling tool or a CRM like Freshsales that includes one. MailerLite also includes an AI writing assistant on the Comfort plan and above, but it is focused on email copy - not lead scoring or deal insights.

## Setup effort and ownership

Freshsales is a moderate implementation decision. The free plan lets a contractor start without cost, and the interface is cleaner than most legacy CRMs. But setup still requires decisions about contact fields, deal stages, pipeline structure, tags, users, and workflow rules. The office needs to answer:

- What are our pipeline stages?
- What fields matter on a contact and deal record?
- Who owns each lead?
- When should we use email, phone, or task follow-up?
- Where do quotes live?
- Which field service or accounting system still owns the job?

MailerLite has a lighter start. The setup questions are list hygiene, consent, segments, forms, landing pages, campaign timing, templates, and reporting. Those still matter, but they do not require the same full customer-lifecycle design. One person can usually own MailerLite as a marketing layer.

The risk is ownership. MailerLite only works if someone writes campaigns, keeps lists clean, builds forms, reviews reports, and sends consistently. Freshsales only works if someone owns CRM process, pipeline hygiene, call accountability, and deal updates. Software will not fix a follow-up process nobody owns.

## Contractor operations fit

Neither Freshsales nor MailerLite should be sold internally as job management software.

Freshsales can manage the sales pipeline: contacts, deals, call logs, tasks, appointments, and follow-up. That can make it feel close to operations software. But it is not a replacement for dispatch, technician routing, work orders, inventory, job costing, crew scheduling, production tracking, or field mobile workflows.

MailerLite is even more clearly a marketing layer. It helps contractors communicate with leads and customers through campaigns, automations, forms, landing pages, and subscriber segments. It does not run estimates, schedules, jobs, invoices, routes, or crews.

A common contractor stack could be: field service software for jobs and scheduling, Freshsales for CRM-led sales follow-up, or MailerLite for campaign-led marketing follow-up. The right answer depends on which gap is real. ## Where Freshsales falls short

Freshsales's biggest limitation in this comparison is email marketing depth. It has email templates and basic sequences, but it is not built for newsletter design, A/B testing, landing page builders, subscriber growth tools, or campaign reporting. If the contractor mainly needs to send polished campaigns and build forms, MailerLite is the better fit.

The second limitation is integration limits on the free plan. Only one third-party integration is included, which means connecting Freshsales to QuickBooks, Jobber, or Zapier requires upgrading to Growth at $9 per user per month.

The third limitation is operations fit. Freshsales can help a contractor win work. It does not run the work. If the office expects CRM to become dispatch, job costing, inventory, and invoicing, the buying process is pointed at the wrong category.

## Where MailerLite falls short

MailerLite's biggest limitation is CRM depth. It can manage subscribers and automation rules, but it is not where most contractors should manage owners, open opportunities, quote stages, expected close dates, lead source attribution, or sales accountability.

The second limitation is workflow handoff. MailerLite can create and nurture demand, but the sales record still has to live somewhere. If the office sends great follow-up emails but still loses active quotes in inboxes and notebooks, MailerLite will not fix the root problem.

The third limitation is list-based pricing. The free plan is useful, and the first paid tiers are affordable at small list sizes, but pricing scales with subscriber count and plan level. A contractor with a large list should price the exact subscriber band before assuming MailerLite stays cheap.

## How to choose

Start with the missing object in the business.

If the missing object is a customer record with owner, stage, source, quote status, next action, and call accountability, Freshsales is the better fit. The free plan and low paid tiers make it easy to test CRM discipline before committing.

If the missing object is a contact list that receives consistent emails, forms, landing pages, and basic automations, MailerLite is the better fit. It gives contractors a lower-risk way to improve marketing communication without rebuilding the sales process.

If both are missing, do not buy both on day one. Decide which leak is more expensive. A contractor losing active quotes because nobody owns the next call should fix CRM first. A contractor with a stable sales process but no reactivation or nurture should fix email first.

## Final verdict

Freshsales is the stronger choice for contractors that need CRM-led follow-up. It makes the most sense when lead source, pipeline stage, call logs, deal value, and next actions should live in one system. The free plan with built-in phone and AI lead scoring makes it a low-risk way to test sales process before paying.

MailerLite is the stronger choice for contractors that need campaign-led follow-up. It makes the most sense when the business has a list, old estimates, past customers, or seasonal messages that need consistent email, forms, landing pages, and automations. The free plan and low paid tiers make it easy to test.

My practical call: choose <a href="/go/freshsales-official-site/" rel="nofollow sponsored">Freshsales</a> if your follow-up problem starts with the sales pipeline. Choose <a href="/go/mailerlite-official-site/" rel="nofollow sponsored">MailerLite</a> if your follow-up problem starts with the list. If the issue is scheduling, dispatch, production, or invoices, skip both for now and look at a contractor operations platform first.
