# AMAG Technology vs Jobber Comparison

<p>On the surface, comparing AMAG Technology with Jobber seems odd. AMAG is an enterprise physical security and access control platform built around Symmetry Access Control. Jobber is a field service management tool for small home service businesses. They share no features, no pricing model, and no typical buyer.</p>

<p>But there's a real comparison question here. It comes up when a low voltage or security contractor realizes the business needs both types of tools and has to decide which one to buy first. The answer isn't which product is better. It's which problem is more urgent today. See our <a href="/best-low-voltage-security-software/">AMAG Technology coverage</a> and <a href="/jobber-review/">Jobber review</a> for detailed looks at each platform.</p>

## The real comparison: two different buying problems

This section exists because the wrong question leads to the wrong shortlist. If a low voltage contractor searches "AMAG vs Jobber" without knowing these products solve different problems, the search result makes it look like a choice. It's not a choice. It's a sequence.

AMAG Technology belongs in the security-system conversation. Its Symmetry Access Control pages position the product around access control that scales, integrations, centralized security management, data-driven decisions, visitor management, identity management, video, PSIM, analytics, door controllers, and card readers. Symmetry Professional is positioned for medium to large enterprise organizations, with support for up to 512 card readers and up to nine clients. Symmetry Enterprise is positioned for a large building, campus, or global multi-site organization, with unlimited card readers, cardholders, and clients listed on the official summary.

Jobber belongs in the field service management conversation. Its Core, Connect, Grow, and Plus tiers serve small residential service contractors who need scheduling, quoting, invoicing, client communication, payment collection, and basic job tracking. Jobber has no access control features because that's not what it's built for.

The only shared buyer is a low voltage or security integrator who installs access control hardware AND runs a service business with technicians, dispatchers, and office staff. That buyer needs both tool categories, but not from the same product. For broader context, use the <a href="/best-low-voltage-security-software/">low voltage and security software roundup</a> alongside the <a href="/field-service-software-alternatives/">field service software alternatives guide</a> instead of treating AMAG and Jobber as direct substitutes.

## Pricing: comparing two entirely different cost models

The pricing gap between these products isn't a gap. It's a category difference.

**AMAG Technology** uses custom project pricing. There's no published price list, no self-serve signup, and no free trial. Cost depends on system scope: software modules, door controllers and readers, cardholder limits, client limits, integrations, support, implementation, upgrade rights, training, and data export. Pricing is handled through AMAG directly or through authorized partners. For a mid-size access control deployment, expect project-level costs rather than monthly SaaS fees. For a small integrator adding a few doors, the cost depends on the partner relationship and project scope.

**Jobber** uses published subscription pricing. Core starts at $29/month with annual billing or $49/month month to month for one user. Connect starts at $99/month annually for one user or $149/month annually for the team version with five users. Grow starts at $149/month annually for one user or $299/month annually for the team version with ten users. Plus starts at $529/month annually and includes fifteen users. Jobber also lists a 14-day no-card trial. The official pricing page checked for this update confirms these rates, and the <a href="/jobber-pricing/">Jobber pricing guide</a> breaks down when the tier jump is worth it.

The buying mistake is comparing these numbers as if they compete. A security integrator should budget for an access control platform AND a field service tool as separate line items, not choose one over the other based on price.

## When to buy AMAG Technology first

Buy AMAG when access control is the project center. If the business sells, installs, and supports Symmetry access control for commercial buildings, schools, hospitals, campuses, or regulated sites, the access control platform is the product, not the overhead. AMAG belongs in the budget before any FSM.

Specific signals that AMAG should come first:

- Your primary revenue comes from access control installations and integrations
- Your projects involve 50+ card readers, multi-site management, or compliance requirements
- You need a platform that supports door controllers, credentials, visitor management, video integration, and event monitoring
- Your customer conversations center on security management, not dispatch scheduling

AMAG also belongs on the shortlist when an integrator is choosing a security platform ecosystem. The Symmetry platform supports integrations, centralized management, analytics, and hardware compatibility that a general FSM can't provide. For adjacent access-control decisions, compare AMAG against <a href="/hid-global-review/">HID Global</a> and the broader <a href="/best-low-voltage-security-software/">low voltage software shortlist</a>.

## When to buy Jobber first

Buy Jobber when daily operations are the bottleneck. If the business needs to organize scheduling, quoting, invoicing, client communication, and payment collection, those problems cost money every day they remain unaddressed. An access control platform won't fix them.

Specific signals that Jobber should come first:

- Technicians are still getting paper instructions or text message schedules
- Quotes and invoices live in separate tools, a notebook, or email threads
- The office spends significant time on manual follow-up and payment chasing
- Customer communication relies on phone calls and sticky notes
- The business has 1-10 technicians and needs a system that works out of the box

Jobber is also the safer first purchase for a contractor that's not yet doing large access control projects. A one-person alarm installer or a two-person low voltage crew can run on Jobber for scheduling and billing while they build the access control side of the business. If the decision is only about field service operations, compare <a href="/jobber-vs-housecall-pro/">Jobber vs Housecall Pro</a> or scan the <a href="/jobber-alternatives/">Jobber alternatives guide</a> before adding security-platform requirements to the shortlist.

## Where they overlap (and where they don't)

The overlap is minimal but real in one scenario: a contractor who installs AMAG Symmetry systems and also runs a service business. That contractor needs both tools but for different workflows.

| Workflow | Right tool |
| --- | --- |
| Scheduling a technician to install a door controller | Jobber |
| Configuring cardholder permissions in the access control system | AMAG Symmetry |
| Sending a quote for a new access control project | Jobber |
| Managing 512 card readers across a campus | AMAG Symmetry |
| Invoicing the customer after installation | Jobber |
| Monitoring event logs and video integration | AMAG Symmetry |
| Following up on unpaid invoices | Jobber |
| Managing multi-site visitor access | AMAG Symmetry |

The mistake is expecting either tool to cover the other category. Jobber won't manage your access control database. AMAG Symmetry won't schedule your technicians.

## The buying sequence for a growing low voltage contractor

The safest approach is sequential, not binary.

**Stage 1 - Get the office organized first.** If the service side of the business is disorganized, fix that before adding a complex security platform. Jobber (or another FSM) gives the business scheduling, quoting, invoicing, and client communication. Without these, the access control projects will be poorly supported because technicians, quotes, invoices, and customer follow-up are running on loose processes.

**Stage 2 - Add the security platform when access control projects justify it.** When the business is regularly selling Symmetry or similar access control installations, invest in AMAG Technology. At this stage, the office already runs on an FSM. The access control platform fills the security-specific gap that the FSM can't cover.

**Stage 3 - Integrate workflows between the two.** Once both tools are in place, the contractor runs access control operations through AMAG Symmetry and business operations through Jobber. They don't need to talk to each other for most workflows. The main integration point is the office process: a quote created in Jobber for an access control project, a site survey completed, the AMAG system programmed, the installation scheduled in Jobber, and the invoice sent from Jobber.

## Where each product can be the wrong fit

AMAG Technology can be the wrong fit when the business doesn't do serious access control work. A small alarm monitoring company or a residential low voltage installer that mainly runs wires and cameras doesn't need an enterprise-grade security platform. The overhead, partner pricing, and project-based implementation aren't worth it for small-scale work. Those contractors should look at simpler access control hardware ecosystems and spend their budget on field service software instead.

Jobber can be the wrong fit when the business needs access control capabilities. An integrator whose core work is Symmetry installations and multi-site security management can't run the business on Jobber alone. The scheduling and invoicing side works fine, but the access control side needs a real platform. Trying to manage card readers, credentials, events, and video through a general FSM is like trying to manage a security system through spreadsheets.

Both tools can be wrong for very small operations that aren't yet doing either field service or access control at scale. A solo operator just starting out may not need either investment and should look at basic tools first: a simple calendar, an invoicing app, and manufacturer documentation.

## Integration and workflow fit

AMAG Technology and Jobber don't have a native integration. They don't need one for most workflows because they solve different problems handled by different people in the business.

The natural workflow bridge is the office process. When a Jobber quote converts to a project, the access control scope goes into the AMAG Symmetry system. The installation gets scheduled in Jobber. Field notes and completion updates flow through Jobber back to the office. The administrative and accounting side runs in Jobber. The security management side runs in Symmetry.

For a small integrator with fewer than five employees, the same person may handle both sides. That's fine. The tools don't need to talk to each other when one person operates both. For a larger operation with a dedicated security team and a separate service dispatch team, the process should account for handoffs, but the tools themselves don't need integration.

## Make the call on stage, not features

The wrong framing is "AMAG vs Jobber which is better." The right framing is "what is the business bottleneck today and which category of tool solves it."

If the business is losing money on missed appointments, slow quotes, unpaid invoices, and manual follow-up, the bottleneck is operations. Buy Jobber or another FSM. The access control projects will benefit from having organized operations underneath them. If the choice is between light FSM and enterprise FSM, use the <a href="/servicetitan-vs-jobber/">ServiceTitan vs Jobber comparison</a> instead of forcing AMAG into the software shortlist.

If the business is losing access control projects to competitors who offer better security platforms, or if the current approach to managing doors, credentials, and events is unprofessional, the bottleneck is the security platform. Buy AMAG or a competing access control system. The field service side can run on whatever the business already has.

If both bottlenecks hurt, buy in sequence. Operations first, security platform second. Most small contractors need to organize their business before they need an enterprise access control platform. But for those who have outgrown both categories, the answer is both products, not one or the other.
