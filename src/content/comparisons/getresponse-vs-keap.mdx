# GetResponse vs Keap: Email Marketing or CRM for Contractors?

GetResponse and Keap both help contractors follow up with leads and keep customer communication from falling through the cracks. But they aren't built for the same buyer, and the difference isn't subtle.

GetResponse is an email marketing and automation platform. Its center of gravity is the contact list: campaigns, newsletters, landing pages, signup forms, automations, webinars, and audience segmentation. It can send better emails than Keap. It can't track a deal through a sales pipeline, send a quote, create an invoice, or process a payment.

Keap is a CRM and marketing automation platform. Its center of gravity is the customer record: CRM, email marketing, text marketing, sales pipeline, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, appointments, and payment processing. It can run more of the lead-to-cash workflow than GetResponse. It costs more, requires implementation, and doesn't have webinars or the same email marketing depth. It's a strong contender in the [best CRM for contractors](/best-crm-for-contractors/) field, especially for offices that want one system instead of many.

My call: choose <a href="/go/getresponse-official-site/" rel="nofollow sponsored">GetResponse</a> if the missing piece is customer communication through campaigns, forms, landing pages, and automated nurture. Choose <a href="/go/keap-official-site/" rel="nofollow sponsored">Keap</a> if the missing piece is the entire lead-to-cash workflow: CRM, pipeline, automated email and text follow-up, quotes, invoices, and payments in one system.

## Quick verdict

Pick GetResponse if the contractor office needs a marketing layer. Its official pages position the platform around email marketing, newsletters, autoresponders, visual marketing automation, landing pages, signup forms, popups, audience segmentation, web push, ecommerce tools, webinars, websites, and creator features. For contractors, that maps to old-estimate nurture, seasonal service reminders, past-customer reactivation, maintenance campaigns, neighborhood landing pages, and educational webinar follow-up.

Pick Keap if the contractor office needs a business-operations layer. Its official pricing page describes CRM, email marketing, text marketing, lead source tracking, sales pipeline, appointment scheduling, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, and payment processing inside one platform. For contractors, that maps to lead capture, automated follow-up, deal tracking, quote delivery, invoice collection, appointment booking, and customer nurture all tied to the same contact record.

Skip both if the real problem is field production. Neither product replaces [Jobber](/jobber-review/), [Housecall Pro](/housecall-pro-review/), [ServiceTitan](/servicetitan-review/), or a broader [field service software](/best-field-service-software/) platform. GetResponse and Keap help before and around the sale. They don't schedule crews, dispatch technicians, track materials, manage routes, or run field invoices.

## When GetResponse makes more sense

GetResponse makes more sense when the contractor has a communication problem. The business may already have names and emails from website quote forms, inspection requests, trade show conversations, financing discussions, old estimates, past customers, referral partners, or seasonal maintenance reminders. The issue is those contacts sit idle because nobody has a reliable way to send useful campaigns.

The official GetResponse feature pages describe email marketing, autoresponders, visual marketing automation, landing pages, forms, popups, segmentation, newsletters, web push, ecommerce automation, webinars, websites, and reporting. In contractor terms, those features support practical follow-up work:

- send a quote-request sequence after a form submission
- build a landing page for one service, neighborhood, or seasonal offer
- remind past customers about maintenance, inspections, upgrades, or warranties
- reactivate old estimates before the busy season
- segment contacts by trade, location, service interest, lead source, or customer type
- invite leads or past customers to an educational webinar
- track campaign opens, clicks, and responses before the next send

GetResponse is strongest when the buyer has a list to communicate with. It's weaker when the buyer needs a source of truth for open deals, owners, stages, activities, quotes, and close probability. A contact list and an active sales pipeline aren't the same object. For a broader look at where GetResponse fits among email tools, see the [best email marketing software for contractors](/best-email-marketing-software-contractors/) roundup.

## When Keap makes more sense

Keap makes more sense when the contractor has a lead-to-cash workflow problem. Leads arrive, but follow-up depends on memory. Quotes go out, but nobody tracks whether the prospect opened them. Invoices sit unpaid because nobody automated a reminder. The owner can't see which deals are stuck or which leads went cold.

The official Keap pricing page describes CRM, automation, email marketing, text marketing, sales pipeline, appointment scheduling, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payment processing, lead source tracking, lead scoring, reporting, and integrations - all inside one platform. For contractors, the practical value is that sales, marketing, and billing actions can live around the same contact record instead of being split across Mailchimp, a spreadsheet, QuickBooks, and a basic CRM.

A remodeling company, roofing company, or specialty trade could use Keap to:

- capture a quote request through a form
- tag the lead by service interest or source
- send an automated email sequence
- create a follow-up task for the office
- send appointment links synced to a calendar
- send a quote the prospect can accept with a button
- convert the quote to an invoice
- collect payment through the platform
- keep nurture campaigns running for old leads and past customers
- track every deal through pipeline stages

That's a broader workflow than GetResponse is trying to cover. GetResponse can send the campaigns. Keap can send the campaigns and run more of the surrounding customer lifecycle.

## Pricing comparison

The price difference is significant, and it reflects the scope difference.

Atlas verifies GetResponse pricing at the 1,000-contact level. A free account or free version is available after the 14-day premium trial. Paid plans start with Starter at $19 per month on monthly billing or $15.58 per month when billed annually. Marketer is $59 per month monthly or $48.38 per month annually. Creator is $69 per month monthly or $56.58 per month annually. Enterprise is custom pricing. The cost driver is contact list size and plan level, not user count.

Atlas verifies Keap pricing as one full-platform subscription starting at $299 per month billed monthly for 2 users and 1,500 contacts. Additional users are $39 per month each. Text marketing Tier 1 is included, with higher tiers at $24, $39, $79, $139, and $279 per month. Optional text message add-on is $6 per month and local number add-on is $10 per month. Usage overages are $0.015 per text message and $0.01 per voice minute. Keap also says implementation services are required, which adds a one-time cost to the true first-year investment.

## Email marketing and automation depth

GetResponse wins the email marketing comparison because email and marketing automation are the product's center of gravity. The official feature page describes a drag-and-drop email creator, newsletters, autoresponders, automation workflows, landing pages, signup forms, popups, audience segmentation, reporting, A/B testing (Marketer and above), contact scoring and tagging (Marketer and above), abandoned cart recovery (Marketer and above), web push notifications, and multichannel marketing features.

For contractors, that makes GetResponse a better fit for nurture campaigns:

- old estimate follow-up
- roof inspection education
- HVAC maintenance reminders
- remodeling planning sequences
- seasonal service campaigns
- financing or promotion announcements
- past-customer newsletters
- referral partner communication

Keap handles email marketing, but its role is different. The official pricing page lists email broadcasts, one-to-one email messages, newsletters, AI content assistant, and AI automation assistant inside the CRM. Email tools support the customer record rather than existing as a standalone marketing platform. That helps when campaigns should trigger from CRM activity like a deal stage change or a new lead form. It's not the same as choosing a dedicated email marketing platform built for deliverability, list growth, segmentation, webinars, and landing page conversion.

Decision signal: if campaigns are the job, GetResponse is the better tool. If campaigns are one part of a larger lead-to-cash workflow, Keap puts them in the right context. For a different angle, see [GetResponse vs Pipedrive](/getresponse-vs-pipedrive/) to compare email marketing against a pure sales pipeline CRM.

## CRM and sales pipeline

Keap wins the CRM depth comparison by a wide margin. GetResponse can store contacts, segment audiences, trigger automation, and support lead nurture. But a contractor sales manager usually needs more than a marketing contact record. They need to know who owns the lead, what stage the deal is in, what happened last, and what has to happen next.

Keap puts that work at the center of the product. The official pricing page lists CRM, contact management, contact segmentation with tags, contact lists and filters, sales pipeline, lead scoring, custom fields, company records, automation history, and a customizable dashboard. Every contact can have a deal stage, an owner, notes, email history, tasks, appointment history, quotes, invoices, and payment records attached.

For a contractor, that means:

- every lead has an owner
- every deal has a stage
- every quote has a follow-up date
- every salesperson can see overdue tasks
- every manager can see pipeline value and stuck opportunities
- the same system that sends the campaign also tracks the deal

GetResponse doesn't have that. It can help before and after those stages with email education and reactivation. But it shouldn't be treated as the main sales pipeline for a team that needs owners, deal stages, due dates, and manager reporting.

Decision signal: Keap if pipeline visibility is the job. GetResponse if email supports the pipeline but doesn't need to become the CRM. For a CRM-only comparison, see [Keap vs Pipedrive](/keap-vs-pipedrive/).

## Text marketing

Keap has built-in text marketing. GetResponse doesn't.

This matters for contractors because text messages get read faster than emails. A quote follow-up, appointment reminder, or seasonal promotion sent by text can reach a homeowner before they check their inbox. Keap's official pricing page lists text marketing with six tiers: Tier 1 is included with 500 messages and 100 voice minutes, and higher tiers go up to $279 per month for 25,000 messages and 2,000 minutes. The page notes text marketing features are available in the US only.

For contractors, text marketing inside the CRM means:

- automated text follow-up when a lead form is submitted
- appointment reminders sent by text with the CRM calendar synced
- text broadcasts to segmented contact lists
- reply handling from the desktop or mobile app
- a dedicated business phone line for calls and texts

GetResponse doesn't offer text marketing as a core feature. If SMS follow-up is important to your sales process, that's a clear point for Keap.

Decision signal: Keap if text follow-up is part of the workflow. GetResponse if email is the primary communication channel. If text marketing is the main draw, [ActiveCampaign vs Keap](/activecampaign-vs-keap/) compares Keap against another automation-heavy alternative.

## Quotes, invoices, and payments

Keap has built-in quotes, invoices, checkout forms, and payment processing. GetResponse doesn't.

Keap's official pricing page describes quotes that prospects can review and accept, invoices that sync with payment and accounting tools, checkout forms that trigger automations, order bumps, promo codes, a native payment processor for US users, and third-party payment processor support for Stripe and PayPal.

For contractors, that means a quote can be created, sent, accepted, and converted to an invoice inside the same platform that tracks the deal and sends the follow-up campaigns. The lead doesn't leave the system between first contact and payment.

GetResponse has ecommerce features including abandoned cart recovery, stores, products, payment gateways, and order confirmation automations. Those are built for online selling, not for a contractor sending a custom quote for a roof replacement or kitchen remodel. If quote and invoice workflow is part of your buying decision, Keap is the clear winner here.

Decision signal: Keap if quotes, invoices, and payment collection need to live in the CRM. GetResponse if those functions are handled elsewhere. For a lighter CRM comparison, [Keap vs MailerLite](/keap-vs-mailerlite/) compares Keap against a simpler email tool.

## Webinars and content marketing

GetResponse has built-in webinar hosting. Keap doesn't.

GetResponse includes live, on-demand, and paid webinars on the Creator and Enterprise plans. The official pricing page describes webinar recording storage, attendee limits (100 on Creator, 1,000 on Enterprise), breakout rooms (Enterprise), and webinar funnels. The Creator plan also includes a website builder, course creator with zero percent transaction fees, and premium newsletter subscriptions.

For contractors, webinars can support an educational sales process. A roofing company might host a webinar on insurance claims. A remodeling company might run a session on kitchen budget planning. A trade show follow-up could include a recorded product demo. If that kind of content marketing is part of the strategy, GetResponse has a feature Keap can't match.

Keap can mention webinars in automation campaign names on its pricing page, but it doesn't host them. A contractor using Keap for webinars would need a separate tool and would need to pass attendee data back into the CRM manually.

Decision signal: GetResponse if webinars or course content are part of the marketing plan. Keap if those aren't relevant. For a broader email-only comparison, see [GetResponse vs Moosend](/getresponse-vs-moosend/).

## Implementation and ease of setup

GetResponse is easier to test. A contractor can sign up for a free account or a 14-day premium trial, import a contact list, create a landing page, build a simple automation, and know within a week whether the team will use it. The official pricing page shows monthly or annual billing options with no implementation requirement.

Keap is a setup project. The official pricing page says required implementation services help users start with strategy consulting, data import, migration, and done-for-you services. That isn't a hidden cost. It's stated on the pricing page. It signals that Keap is powerful enough that configuration matters, and that the platform touches enough of the business process that a bad setup will waste the subscription.

If your office is still debating what a lead stage should be called, GetResponse is the safer starting point. If you already know your lifecycle stages and want automation built around them, Keap's heavier onboarding is the price of a more complete system.

## Contractor operations fit

Neither platform should be treated as field service software.

GetResponse can help with newsletters, landing pages, forms, popups, automated nurture, webinars, segmentation, customer reactivation, and campaign reporting. It won't create estimates, dispatch crews, schedule technicians, track inventory, manage routes, collect job photos, or run job costing.

Keap can help with CRM, email, text marketing, sales pipeline, appointment links, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payments, and automation campaigns. It won't manage work orders, crew calendars, technician routing, materials, production tracking, or field invoices.

That separation can be useful. A contractor might use Keap for the lead-to-cash workflow, GetResponse for broader marketing communication and webinars, and a field service or project system for the actual work. The danger is expecting one front-office tool to do all three jobs.

## Where GetResponse falls short

GetResponse's biggest limitation in this comparison is CRM and business workflow depth. It can support contacts, segments, forms, landing pages, and automation paths, but It's not where a contractor should manage every open quote, owner, stage, probability, and next activity. It can't send a quote, create an invoice, or process a payment. If the office needs a sales board with deal tracking and billing workflow, Keap is the more natural fit.

The second limitation is text marketing. GetResponse doesn't offer SMS as a core feature. Contractors who rely on text follow-up for appointment reminders, quote delivery, or lead reactivation will need a separate tool or should choose Keap.

The third limitation is ownership. Email marketing only pays off if someone keeps lists clean, writes campaigns, reviews reports, tests forms, and maintains automations. A free account path doesn't matter if nobody owns communication.

## Where Keap falls short

Keap's biggest drawback is price. A $299 per month billed-monthly starting point changes the buying decision. It's not a cheap experiment. It's got to replace enough manual follow-up, missed leads, separate email tools, quote workflow, invoice workflow, and customer communication to justify the spend.

The second drawback is implementation. Keap's own pricing page says required implementation services help users start with strategy consulting, data import, migration, and done-for-you services. Contractors who want a CRM by Friday may find Keap too much. The setup takes time, and the configuration decisions matter.

The third drawback is email marketing depth. Keap can send broadcasts, newsletters, and automated emails. But It's not a dedicated email marketing platform in the same way GetResponse is. Contractors looking for advanced segmentation, A/B testing, web push, webinars, and deep deliverability tools should evaluate GetResponse first.

The fourth drawback is webinar and content marketing. Keap doesn't host webinars or offer a course creator. If educational content marketing is part of the strategy, that feature gap matters.

## Can you use both?

Yes, and for some contractors that's the cleanest setup. Keap can own the lead-to-cash workflow: CRM, pipeline, quotes, invoices, text follow-up, and payment collection. GetResponse can own broader marketing communication: newsletters, seasonal campaigns, landing pages, webinars, and past-customer reactivation.

A practical stack could look like this:

1. Website lead enters Keap as a contact and deal.
2. Keap sends an automated email and text follow-up sequence.
3. Estimator moves the opportunity through pipeline stages.
4. Keap sends a quote the prospect can accept.
5. Won work moves into the field service or project system.
6. Lost quotes, old leads, and past customers sync into GetResponse segments.
7. GetResponse handles newsletters, maintenance reminders, reactivation campaigns, educational sequences, and webinar invites.

That setup only works if the handoff is documented. Someone has to decide which contacts move into GetResponse, which records stay in Keap, how consent is handled, and how unsubscribes are respected across both platforms. Without those rules, the company can end up with two disconnected databases and no clear owner.

## Final recommendation

Choose GetResponse if the business has a marketing communication gap. It's the better fit for newsletters, old-estimate nurture, past-customer reactivation, landing pages, forms, webinars, and automated campaigns. The free account path and lower first paid plan make it easier to start with one marketing owner.

Choose Keap if the business has a lead-to-cash workflow gap. It's the better fit when leads, quotes, invoices, payments, text follow-up, and pipeline management all need to live together in one system. The higher price only makes sense if those workflows replace manual office work or multiple disconnected tools.

If you're not sure which gap is larger, audit the last 30 days of missed follow-up. If most misses were people who should have received a campaign, start with GetResponse. If most misses were active opportunities nobody owned, quotes nobody followed up on, or invoices nobody collected, start with Keap.

<p><strong>Bottom line:</strong> GetResponse helps contractors communicate with a list. Keap helps contractors run the entire lead-to-cash process. The better choice is the one that fixes the specific bottleneck in your office.</p>
