# Keap vs MailerLite for Contractor Follow-Up

Keap and MailerLite both help contractors follow up with leads and customers, but they sit in different parts of the business. Keap is a CRM and marketing automation platform. MailerLite is an email marketing and automation platform.

That sounds like a small difference until you map the day-to-day work. Keap is for the contractor that wants a customer record, pipeline stages, lead source tracking, appointment links, quotes, invoices, payments, email, and text messaging tied together. MailerLite is for the contractor that wants a clean way to send campaigns, build forms, publish landing pages, run simple automations, and keep a contact list warm.

## Quick verdict

Pick Keap if the contractor office needs one place to manage the customer journey before a job becomes field work. The official Keap pricing page positions the current platform around CRM, contact management, tags, sales pipeline, appointment scheduling, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payment workflow, email marketing, text marketing, forms, landing pages, and required implementation services. For a contractor, that points to lead intake, nurture, quote follow-up, and payment communication living around the same customer record.

Pick MailerLite if the contractor office already has a contact list, a website, or a basic CRM, but follow-up is inconsistent. The official MailerLite pricing and feature pages position the product around newsletters, email automations, landing pages, websites, forms, pop-ups, subscriber segmentation, reporting, and affordable plans. For a contractor, that points to campaigns: past-customer reactivation, seasonal reminders, quote follow-up emails, and simple lead capture.

Skip both if the problem is field operations. Neither product replaces [Jobber](/jobber-review/), [Housecall Pro](/housecall-pro-review/), [ServiceTitan](/servicetitan-review/), or a broader [field service software](/field-service-software-alternatives/) platform. Keap and MailerLite can help before and around the sale. They do not dispatch crews, route technicians, track production, manage job costing, or run field workflows by themselves.

## When Keap makes more sense

Keap makes more sense when the follow-up problem is tied to sales process ownership. A lead comes in from a form. Someone needs to call. Someone needs to send an appointment link. Someone needs to move the opportunity through a pipeline, send a quote, follow up after the quote, collect payment, and keep the customer record clean.

That is broader than email marketing. Keap's official pricing page lists CRM, contact management, contact segmentation with tags, contact lists and filters, lead source tracking, sales pipeline, appointment scheduler, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payment processing, email marketing, and text marketing. The contractor value is not that Keap sends an email. It is that the email, text, appointment, quote, and invoice can sit closer to the same customer record.

A roofing, remodeling, HVAC, or specialty trade contractor might use Keap to:

- capture a quote request through a form
- tag the lead by service line, source, or urgency
- send automated email or text follow-up
- create a task for the office or estimator
- send an appointment link
- send a quote and turn it into an invoice
- keep a past-customer nurture campaign connected to the CRM record

That connected workflow is the reason to consider Keap. The tradeoff is cost and setup. Atlas verifies the current official pricing render as starting at $299 per month billed monthly for 2 users and 1,500 contacts, with required implementation services linked from the pricing page. Additional users, text tiers, and add-ons can raise the real monthly cost.

## When MailerLite makes more sense

MailerLite makes more sense when the contractor has a list problem or a campaign problem. The business may already have names and emails from old estimates, past customers, seasonal maintenance work, website forms, trade shows, financing inquiries, or referrals. The problem is that those contacts rarely receive useful follow-up.

MailerLite is built for that layer. Its official pages describe newsletters, email automations, landing pages, websites, forms, pop-ups, segmentation, reporting, A/B testing, and a drag-and-drop email editor. For a contractor, that maps to practical marketing work:

- send a quote follow-up sequence after an estimate request
- reactivate old estimates before a seasonal promotion
- remind past customers about maintenance, inspections, warranties, or upgrades
- create a landing page for one service or neighborhood
- collect new subscribers with forms and pop-ups
- segment contacts by service interest, location, source, or customer type
- review campaign reports before the next send

That does not make MailerLite a replacement for a CRM. It can store subscribers, tags, forms, and automation paths, but it is not the natural place to manage every open quote, owner, deal value, probability, estimator assignment, or won/lost reason. The best MailerLite buyer says: we need to communicate better with people we already know.

## Pricing comparison

The pricing gap is large enough to decide the first round for many small contractor offices.

Atlas verifies Keap's current official pricing as one full-platform subscription starting at $299 per month billed monthly for 2 users and 1,500 contacts. The official page also says required implementation services help with strategy consulting, data import, migration, and done-for-you services. Atlas records additional users at $39 per month per user. Text marketing Tier 1 is included, with higher text tiers and text add-ons available.

Atlas verifies MailerLite's official pricing as a free plan at $0 for up to 250 subscribers and 2,500 monthly emails. At the 500-subscriber level, Comfort is $12 per month, or $10.80 per month when billed yearly. Power is $25 per month, or $22.50 per month when billed yearly. Enterprise is custom for businesses with more than 200,000 subscribers.

| Scenario | Keap | MailerLite |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lowest published entry | $299/mo billed monthly for 2 users and 1,500 contacts | Free for up to 250 subscribers and 2,500 monthly emails |
| First paid tier | Same platform subscription starting at $299/mo monthly | Comfort from $10.80/mo annual or $12/mo monthly at 500 subscribers |
| Higher costs | Additional users, implementation services, text tiers, text add-ons, and usage | Higher subscriber counts, Power plan, Enterprise, and optional add-ons |
| Billing unit | Platform subscription plus users, contacts, and messaging needs | Subscribers, email volume, and marketing plan level |
| Budget fit | Contractors replacing multiple front-office tools | Contractors adding a focused email layer |

The simple rule: Keap has to replace enough manual office work to justify the cost. MailerLite can be justified as a focused marketing tool much earlier because the entry point is lower and the free plan is real.

## CRM and sales pipeline depth

Keap wins the CRM comparison. It is closer to a front-office customer system. Contact records, tags, lead source tracking, pipeline stages, appointment links, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payments, email, and text messages can all sit in one workflow.

For contractors, that matters when follow-up depends on status. A new roof replacement lead may need one response. A quoted-but-not-sold lead may need another. A customer who accepted a quote but has not paid may need a different message. A past customer may need a seasonal reminder. Keap is stronger when those actions should depend on the CRM record and sales stage.

MailerLite does not compete there directly. It can segment subscribers and trigger automations, but its record is a marketing contact record, not a full sales pipeline record. That is fine if another system owns sales accountability. It is a problem if the contractor expects MailerLite to become the office's lead, quote, and estimator command center.

Decision signal: choose Keap when the question is "who owns this lead and what stage is it in?" Choose MailerLite when the question is "which list or segment should receive this campaign?" If the Keap side is the fixed point, compare the deeper sales-CRM tradeoff in [Keap vs Pipedrive](/keap-vs-pipedrive/).

## Email marketing and campaign work

MailerLite wins the email marketing comparison for a contractor that wants focused campaign execution. The product is designed around emails, landing pages, forms, automations, subscriber lists, reports, and audience growth. That makes it easier for a small office or marketing coordinator to start with a simple workflow.

A practical MailerLite setup could look like this:

1. A landing page captures storm-damage inspection leads.
2. A form tags the subscriber by service area.
3. A five-email sequence explains the inspection process.
4. A separate campaign goes to old estimates before peak season.
5. Reports show which subject lines, forms, and links performed.

Keap can also send email and text campaigns. Its advantage is that those campaigns can be tied to the CRM record. Its disadvantage is that the system is heavier. If the contractor just needs a clean newsletter tool, MailerLite will feel faster. If the contractor needs campaigns to trigger from pipeline movement, quote activity, or customer record updates, Keap is the stronger fit.

## Setup effort and ownership

Keap is a bigger implementation decision. The official pricing page says required implementation services help users start with strategy consulting, data import, migration, and done-for-you services. That is a useful signal. Keap can touch many parts of the customer journey, so setup quality matters.

A contractor choosing Keap should map the workflow before buying:

- Where does a lead enter?
- What fields and tags matter?
- What are the pipeline stages?
- When should the office use email, text, phone, or appointment links?
- Where are quotes and invoices created today?
- What should happen after a quote is accepted?
- Which field service or accounting system still owns the job?

MailerLite has a lighter start. The setup questions are list hygiene, consent, segments, forms, landing pages, campaign timing, templates, and reporting. Those still matter, but they do not require the same full customer-lifecycle design. One person can usually own MailerLite as a marketing layer.

The risk is ownership. MailerLite only works if someone writes campaigns, keeps lists clean, builds forms, reviews reports, and sends consistently. Keap only works if someone owns CRM process, pipeline hygiene, automation rules, and handoffs. Software will not fix a follow-up process nobody owns.

For a second angle on the email-tool-versus-CRM decision, the [MailerLite vs Pipeline CRM](/mailerlite-vs-pipeline-crm/) comparison separates list nurture from active sales pipeline management.

## Contractor operations fit

Neither Keap nor MailerLite should be sold internally as job management software.

Keap can handle a broader front-office workflow than MailerLite: CRM, appointment links, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payments, email, and text. That can make it feel close to operations software. But it still is not a replacement for dispatch, technician routing, work orders, inventory, job costing, crew scheduling, production tracking, or field mobile workflows.

MailerLite is even more clearly a marketing layer. It helps contractors communicate with leads and customers through campaigns, automations, forms, landing pages, and subscriber segments. It does not run estimates, schedules, jobs, invoices, routes, or crews.

A common stack could be: field service software for jobs, Keap for CRM-led follow-up, or MailerLite for campaign-led follow-up. The right answer depends on which gap is real.

## Best fit by contractor type

| Contractor situation | Better fit | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Owner wants one customer system for lead intake, pipeline, quotes, and follow-up | Keap | CRM record and customer workflow matter more than simple campaigns |
| Small contractor has an old customer list and no newsletter rhythm | MailerLite | Lower-cost campaign tooling solves the direct problem |
| Sales team needs stages, owners, next actions, and lead source tracking | Keap | Pipeline and contact records are central |
| One marketing coordinator owns emails, landing pages, and forms | MailerLite | Campaign workflow is easier to start and cheaper to test |
| Company already has Jobber, Housecall Pro, or ServiceTitan for operations | Depends | Use Keap if CRM follow-up is missing; use MailerLite if email nurture is missing |
| Budget is tight and the list is small | MailerLite | Free and low paid plans reduce the risk of testing |
| Business wants text messaging tied to CRM activity | Keap | Text marketing is part of Keap's broader customer workflow |
| Main problem is dispatch or job costing | Neither | Start with field service or project management software instead |

## Where Keap falls short

Keap's biggest drawback in this comparison is cost. A $299 per month monthly starting point is not a casual email tool purchase. It has to replace enough office labor, missed follow-up, disconnected quote workflow, and customer communication to make sense.

The second drawback is setup effort. Keap can connect more pieces because it touches more pieces. That means more decisions about fields, tags, pipelines, forms, campaigns, quote workflow, invoice workflow, text messaging, permissions, and reporting.

The third drawback is fit. Contractors who mainly need monthly newsletters, seasonal reminders, or a simple estimate follow-up sequence may not need a CRM-led automation platform. Buying Keap for basic email is like buying a truck when the job calls for a hand cart.

## Where MailerLite falls short

MailerLite's biggest limitation is CRM depth. It can manage subscribers and automation rules, but it is not where most contractors should manage owners, open opportunities, quote stages, expected close dates, lead source attribution, or sales accountability.

The second limitation is workflow handoff. MailerLite can create and nurture demand, but the sales record still has to live somewhere. If the office sends great follow-up emails but still loses active quotes in inboxes and notebooks, MailerLite will not fix the root problem.

The third limitation is operational scope. A contractor may see forms, landing pages, websites, automations, and emails and assume MailerLite can manage the business. It cannot. It is a communication tool, not a job system.

## How to choose

Start with the missing object in the business.

If the missing object is a customer record with owner, stage, source, quote status, next action, and payment workflow, Keap is the better fit. The price only makes sense when those records and automations replace manual front-office work.

If the missing object is a contact list that receives consistent emails, forms, landing pages, and basic automations, MailerLite is the better fit. It gives contractors a lower-risk way to improve marketing communication without rebuilding the sales process.

If both are missing, do not buy both on day one. Decide which leak is more expensive. A contractor losing active quotes because nobody owns the next call should fix CRM first. A contractor with a stable sales process but no reactivation or nurture should fix email first.

## Final verdict

Keap is the stronger choice for contractors that need CRM-led follow-up. It makes the most sense when lead source, pipeline stage, appointments, quotes, invoices, email, and text messaging should live around the same customer record. The higher starting cost and implementation effort are easier to justify when Keap replaces several disconnected front-office workflows.

MailerLite is the stronger choice for contractors that need campaign-led follow-up. It makes the most sense when the business has a list, old estimates, past customers, or seasonal messages that need consistent email, forms, landing pages, automations, and reporting. The free plan and low paid tiers make it easier to test.

My practical call: choose Keap if your follow-up problem starts with the sales pipeline. Choose MailerLite if your follow-up problem starts with the list.
