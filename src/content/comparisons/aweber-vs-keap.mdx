# AWeber vs Keap: Email Marketing or CRM Automation?

AWeber and Keap both help contractors communicate with leads and customers, but they come from entirely different directions. AWeber is an email marketing platform that has been around since 1998. Keap is a CRM and marketing automation platform that evolved from Infusionsoft. The comparison is not really "which has better features" but "which problem are you solving."

If the pain point is sending professional emails, building landing pages, and running simple follow-up sequences without spending much, AWeber is the natural fit. If the pain point is managing leads through a sales pipeline, automating follow-up based on deal stage, and having quotes, invoices, and text messaging in one system, Keap deserves the look. For AWeber's broader positioning, see where it ranks in the <a href="/best-email-marketing-software-contractors/">best email marketing software for contractors roundup</a>. For Keap, see the <a href="/best-crm-for-contractors/">best CRM for contractors roundup</a>.

## When this software makes sense

Contractors reach for tools like these when the follow-up gap starts costing jobs. Leads come in from the website, some get calls, others get emails, and nobody can say which follow-ups actually produced work. The seasonal promotion goes to the whole list instead of the customers who need that service. The monthly newsletter takes a full afternoon to compose and send.

Both AWeber and Keap solve that problem, but they start from different foundations.

AWeber makes sense when email marketing is the primary need. A contractor running a roofing company who wants every "get a free estimate" website submission to trigger a welcome email, a follow-up with case studies, and a check-in reminder 30 days later can build that in AWeber's automation builder. The landing page builder, form tools, and email templates are the main event. Contact management exists, but it is subscriber-centric, not pipeline-centric.

Keap makes sense when CRM is the primary need. A contractor who wants to see every lead in a sales pipeline, know which stage each deal is at, and automate follow-up based on that stage will find Keap's contact and pipeline management more structured out of the box. Email automation sits on top of the CRM foundation, along with quotes, invoices, text messaging, and payment processing.

## When it does not make sense yet

Skip both if your email needs are basic: a monthly newsletter to a small list of existing customers. A free plan from MailerLite or Mailchimp covers that without paying for automation workflows you will not use. If that sounds closer to your use case, start with the <a href="/best-email-marketing-software-contractors/">email marketing software for contractors roundup</a> before paying for a combined email-CRM platform.

Also skip both if your CRM need is strictly field service management: scheduling, dispatch, estimates, and invoicing. Neither AWeber nor Keap handles job scheduling or dispatch. You would still need a field service platform like <a href="/jobber-review/">Jobber</a>, <a href="/housecall-pro-review/">Housecall Pro</a>, or another option from the <a href="/field-service-software-alternatives/">field service software alternatives guide</a> alongside either of them.

The specific risk with AWeber: if CRM depth matters more than email marketing (structured pipelines, deal stages with probability-weighted forecasts, multi-user sales team workflows), AWeber's contact management will feel limited compared to a dedicated CRM like <a href="/keap-vs-pipedrive/">Pipedrive</a> or <a href="/keap-vs-zoho-crm/">Zoho CRM</a>, or Keap's pipeline foundation.

The specific risk with Keap: the starting price is $299 per month billed monthly. If your primary need is sending professional emails to a subscriber list, Keap is the wrong tool. It is a CRM platform that includes email, not the other way around. For a lighter email-first alternative, compare <a href="/keap-vs-mailerlite/">Keap vs MailerLite</a> or <a href="/activecampaign-vs-keap/">ActiveCampaign vs Keap</a>. For a pure sales pipeline without the marketing overhead, see <a href="/keap-vs-pipedrive/">Keap vs Pipedrive</a>.

AWeber and Keap are easiest to compare when you separate email marketing from CRM and operations. They overlap, but they do not solve the same first problem.

| You need a CRM with sales pipeline, contact records, lead scoring, and deal-stage automation | Keap | CRM is the foundation, with marketing automation layered around the customer record. |
| You mainly need basic monthly email blasts to a small list | Neither | A simpler free email tool covers that without paying for workflows you will not use. |

The practical test: if the next problem to fix is "we need to send better emails more consistently," lean AWeber. If the next problem is "we need to know where every lead is in the pipeline and who owns the next follow-up," lean Keap.

## Email marketing and automation depth

AWeber's email builder is its strongest argument. The drag-and-drop editor, combined with hundreds of pre-built mobile-responsive templates, lets a contractor create a professional-looking email without design experience. A contractor could build: "If someone fills out the quote request form, send a welcome email, wait three days, send a case study, wait another week, send a check-in." The automation builder supports behavioral triggers (opens, clicks, form submissions) and branching logic.

AWeber also supports email split testing, personalized content blocks, AMP emails, and RSS-to-email for automated blog digests. The AI Subject Line Assistant helps with subject line ideas. For a contractor who wants to send regular campaigns without a dedicated marketing person, AWeber's email tooling is accessible and capable.

Keap's email automation follows a different philosophy. Sequences are built around a timeline: add a contact to a campaign, they receive emails in order, with rules for when to advance or exit. It works well for standard workflows like new lead intake, proposal follow-up, and post-job check-ins. The email functionality is capable, but it is designed to serve the CRM record, not to be the primary tool a marketing coordinator picks up first.

Keap also includes an AI Content Assistant for composing emails and an AI Automation Assistant for building campaign templates. Email broadcasts, 1:1 emails (synced with Gmail or Outlook), and newsletters are all available. But if building complex, conditional email sequences with multiple branches and visual drag-and-drop is the main use case, AWeber's email-first approach is more intuitive.

Decision signal: if you plan to build email campaigns as a core marketing activity and want templates, split testing, and a visual builder, AWeber is the clear choice. If your email needs are straightforward follow-up sequences triggered by CRM activity (deal stage changes, form submissions, appointment bookings), Keap gets the job done.

## CRM and contact management

Keap wins the CRM comparison decisively. The contact record includes pipelines, deal stages, lead source tracking, appointment history, notes, custom fields, and tags. The pipeline view shows deals at each stage: new lead, contacted, quoted, negotiating, won, lost. A contractor can drag deals between stages, attach emails and notes to each deal, filter by team member or deal source, and automate actions based on stage changes.

Keap also includes lead scoring, contact segmentation through tags and custom fields, and a customizable dashboard. For a contractor managing a steady flow of inbound leads and wanting a visible sales funnel, Keap's CRM is a real front-office system.

AWeber's contact management is subscriber-centric. Contact records show list membership, tags, engagement data (opens, clicks), and subscription status. AWeber can segment subscribers by tag, custom field, and engagement behavior, but it does not have a sales pipeline, deal stages, deal values, or opportunity tracking. For a contractor who mainly needs to know who is on the email list and who is engaging with campaigns, AWeber's subscriber management is sufficient. For a contractor who needs pipeline management, territory-based sales tracking, or multi-user deal assignment, AWeber will fall short.

Decision signal: Keap if CRM is the center of gravity. AWeber if email marketing is the center and contact management just needs to support list segmentation and campaign targeting.

A contractor paying for Keap needs to replace enough manual follow-up, disconnected tools, and missed pipeline visibility to justify twenty-four times AWeber's entry cost.

The simple rule: AWeber can be justified as a focused email tool almost immediately. Keap needs a clear automation and CRM use case that replaces real office labor.

## Text marketing and SMS

Keap includes text marketing as a core feature. The official pricing page lists six usage tiers: Tier 1 is included with 500 messages and 100 voice minutes, Tier 2 at $24/month for 1,000 messages, up to Tier 6 at $279/month for 25,000 messages. Contractors can send 1:1 text messages, broadcast texts, automated texts, and handle reply conversations. A dedicated business phone line is available. Text overages are $0.015 per message and $0.01 per voice minute.

For contractors, text marketing is a real differentiator. A roofing or HVAC contractor who wants to send appointment reminders, follow up on quotes via SMS, or run a seasonal promotion to a segmented list can do that inside Keap without a separate texting tool.

AWeber does not include text marketing. If SMS is part of the follow-up workflow, a contractor using AWeber would need a separate tool for text messaging, which adds cost and complexity.

Decision signal: if text marketing is part of the follow-up plan, Keap is the only option here. If email is the primary communication channel, AWeber covers that well.

## Quotes, invoices, and payments

Keap includes quote creation, invoicing, checkout forms, order bumps, promo codes, and a native payment processor (available in the U.S.). Third-party payment processors like Stripe, PayPal, and Eway are also supported. For a contractor, this means the entire front-office revenue flow (lead, quote, invoice, payment) can live in one system.

AWeber does not include quotes, invoices, or payment processing as CRM features. AWeber can sell products and services through its ecommerce tools, with transaction fees on sales (0.6% on Plus/Done For You, 1.0% on Lite). But it is not a quoting or invoicing tool.

Decision signal: if the contractor needs quotes, invoices, and payment collection connected to the customer record, Keap is the only option in this comparison. If those functions already live in another system (field service software, accounting software), AWeber's lack of them is not a problem.

## User reviews and reputation

AWeber has been in the email marketing business since 1998. The platform carries a strong reputation for deliverability, customer support (24/7 across all plans), and template quality. Common praise focuses on ease of use, template selection, and reliable email delivery. Common complaints mention limited automation depth compared to newer tools and an interface that feels dated.

Keap holds a G2 rating around 4.1/5 and a Capterra rating around 4.2/5. Praise centers on CRM depth, all-in-one functionality, and automation that keeps sales workflows moving. Complaints focus on setup complexity, a steeper learning curve, and pricing that feels high for smaller contact lists. Keap's review volume is lower than AWeber's, which makes it harder to benchmark against similar small businesses.

Decision signal: AWeber's longer history and higher review volume make it the safer pick if email marketing is the priority. Keap's reviews reflect its CRM strength but the lower scores suggest a more niche fit for contractors who specifically need CRM-led automation.

## Setup effort and ownership

AWeber has a lighter setup. A contractor can create an account, import a subscriber list, pick a template, and send a first campaign in an afternoon. The automation builder is visual and does not require technical skills. The main setup questions are list hygiene, consent, segments, forms, landing pages, and campaign timing.

Keap is a bigger implementation decision. The official pricing page says required implementation services help with strategy consulting, data import, migration, and done-for-you services. A contractor choosing Keap should map the workflow before buying:

- Where does a lead enter?
- What fields and tags matter?
- What are the pipeline stages?
- When should the office use email, text, phone, or appointment links?
- Where are quotes and invoices created today?
- What should happen after a quote is accepted?
- Which field service or accounting system still owns the job?

The risk with both tools is ownership. AWeber only works if someone writes campaigns, keeps lists clean, and sends consistently. Keap only works if someone owns CRM process, pipeline hygiene, and automation rules. Software will not fix a follow-up process nobody owns.

## Contractor operations fit

Neither AWeber nor Keap is field service software. Both sit before or beside operations.

AWeber is a marketing and communication layer. It helps contractors send emails, build forms, publish landing pages, and manage subscriber lists. It does not run estimates, schedules, jobs, invoices, routes, or crews. For a broader view of contractor software categories, see the <a href="/field-service-software-alternatives/">field service software alternatives</a> guide.

Keap covers more of the front-office workflow than AWeber: CRM, appointment links, quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payments, email, and text. That can make it feel close to operations software. But it still is not a replacement for dispatch, technician routing, work orders, inventory, job costing, crew scheduling, or production tracking.

A common contractor stack could be: <a href="/best-field-service-software/">field service software</a> for jobs, plus AWeber for email campaigns or Keap for CRM-led follow-up. The right answer depends on which gap is real.

## AWeber: what stands out

The email templates and drag-and-drop builder are the headline. AWeber offers hundreds of professional, mobile-responsive templates that a contractor can customize without design experience. The email builder supports personalized content blocks, split testing, and AMP emails. For a contractor who wants to send campaigns that look professional without hiring a designer, AWeber makes that accessible.

The free plan is real. Up to 500 subscribers and 3,000 emails per month at zero cost is a genuine starting point. The Lite plan at $12.50 per month annual removes the free-plan limitations and adds automation, landing pages, and advanced analytics. The pricing is transparent and predictable.

The deliverability track record matters. AWeber has been sending email since 1998, and that infrastructure investment shows in deliverability rates. For a contractor whose emails need to reach the inbox, not the spam folder, AWeber's sending reputation is a real advantage.

## Where AWeber falls short

The CRM is shallow. AWeber manages subscribers, tags, and engagement data, but it does not have a sales pipeline, deal stages, deal values, or opportunity tracking. If a contractor needs pipeline visibility and deal-stage accountability, AWeber is the wrong tool.

No text marketing. If SMS follow-up is part of the workflow, a separate texting tool is required, adding cost and complexity.

## Keap: what stands out

CRM is the foundation, not an afterthought. Keap's contact management starts with a structured pipeline: new lead, contacted, quoted, negotiating, won, lost. Each stage can trigger automated actions: send a follow-up email, assign a task, move to a different campaign. For a contractor who wants to see every active deal in one view, Keap gives that structure immediately.

The breadth of the platform is the main draw. Quotes, invoices, checkout forms, payments, email, text, landing pages, forms, appointments, and reporting all live in one system. For a contractor replacing a spreadsheet CRM, a separate email tool, a separate invoicing tool, and a separate texting app, Keap can consolidate all of that.

The text marketing system is built in. With six usage tiers and 1:1, broadcast, and automated texting, a contractor can run SMS follow-up without a third-party tool. That is a meaningful advantage for contractors who know text messages get read more than emails.

Lead scoring is included. Contacts can be scored based on engagement and behavior, helping a contractor prioritize which leads to call first.

## Where Keap falls short

The starting price is high. At $299 per month billed monthly for 2 users and 1,500 contacts, Keap is not a casual experiment. Required implementation services add upfront cost and time. The platform needs to replace real office labor to justify the investment.

The automation builder is less intuitive than AWeber's email-first approach. Keap uses a timeline-and-template approach that works well for standard sequences but requires more effort for non-standard workflows or complex conditional branches. If automation experimentation is the primary use case, AWeber's builder is easier to iterate with.

Setup takes longer. Keap's feature depth means configuring pipelines, campaigns, contact fields, text messaging, quotes, and invoices properly requires upfront planning. The required implementation services reflect that reality.

The review ratings (around 4.1-4.2 on G2 and Capterra) are lower than AWeber's, with complaints about the learning curve, complexity, and pricing for smaller lists. Fewer reviews make it harder to benchmark against similar businesses.

## The bottom line for contractors

AWeber and Keap overlap in the idea of "communicate with leads and customers," but they deliver that from opposite starting points.

AWeber is for the contractor whose pain is email: sending professional campaigns, building landing pages, running follow-up sequences, and managing a subscriber list without spending much. The free plan, low paid tiers, and template library make it accessible at any list size. The tradeoff is that it is not a CRM, does not handle text marketing, and does not manage quotes or invoices.

Keap is for the contractor whose pain is CRM: tracking leads through a pipeline, managing deal stages, and layering email, text, quotes, and invoices on top of that structure. The CRM is the foundation, and the automation supports it. It costs significantly more and takes longer to set up, but for contractors whose primary need is pipeline management and connected front-office workflows, Keap is the more complete system.
