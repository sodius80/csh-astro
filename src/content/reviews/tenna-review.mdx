<p>Tenna is built for a very specific equipment problem: construction companies don't just manage road vehicles. They manage excavators, loaders, cranes, trailers, pickup trucks, attachments, small tools, consumables, parts, rental assets, job-site moves, maintenance history, safety events, and cost questions. Tenna pulls those pieces into one construction-first fleet and equipment system.</p>

<p>That focus is why Tenna sits at the top of our <a href="/best-equipment-tracking-software-contractors/">equipment tracking software for contractors</a> shortlist. The trade-off is price visibility. Atlas verifies Tenna as custom quote, and the official pricing page uses a pricing form rather than public tiers. If you're trying to compare vendors in an afternoon, Tenna won't give you a neat monthly price table. You'll need a quote.</p>

<h2>Right for / Not for</h2>
<div class="right-for">
<h3>Right for you if:</h3>
<ul>
<li>You manage a mixed fleet of heavy equipment, trucks, trailers, attachments, tools, and consumables.</li>
<li>You need location, utilization, maintenance, safety, and dispatching data in one construction context.</li>
<li>You want asset records tied to jobs, sites, cost centers, shops, and field teams.</li>
<li>You already use construction systems that need equipment data, such as ERP, accounting, project operations, or OEM telematics feeds.</li>

<h2>Feature Deep Dive</h2>

<h3>Mixed-asset tracking for real construction fleets</h3>
<p>Tenna's official pricing page asks buyers which asset classes they need to track: heavy equipment, fleet vehicles, mid-sized equipment, small assets and tools, attachments, and consumables or inventory. That matters because a contractor's asset problem rarely stops at trucks. The missing item might be a generator, a trench box, a bucket, a compressor, a plate, a trailer, or a pallet of consumables.</p>

<p>The product uses GPS, BLE, QR, and telematics hardware across those asset types. Tenna's AI information page describes GPS and BLE-based tracking for heavy equipment, on-road vehicles, attachments, mid-sized assets, parts, tools, and consumables. The practical value is a single source for where an asset is, who has it, what job it supports, whether it is being used, and what maintenance history follows it. If your pain is crew time and GPS time cards instead of asset control, compare <a href="/clockshark-review/">ClockShark</a> before buying a fleet system.</p>

<h3>Fleet maps, utilization, and job-site visibility</h3>
<p>The official fleet page describes maps, live data, asset detail, fleet database records, vehicle utilization, job-site assignment, alerts, and analytics dashboards. That is the buying case for Tenna: not just a dot on a map, but a better answer to whether equipment is sitting idle, stuck at the wrong site, due for service, or costing more than it should.</p>

<p>For owners and operations leaders, utilization is the piece that turns tracking into a business case. If one crew is renting equipment while another machine sits idle across town, location data alone doesn't fix it. You need asset status, assignment history, cost data, and enough reporting to make the next rental or transfer decision with facts instead of phone calls.</p>

<h3>Maintenance, work orders, parts, and shop workflows</h3>
<p>Tenna's official materials list preventive maintenance, work orders, mechanic time cards, parts inventory, cost codes, fault-code handling, repair documentation, and maintenance alerts. That is a big reason Tenna is a different buying decision from a basic tracker. The question isn't only where the asset is. It's whether the asset is ready, what repair is open, what part is needed, who touched it last, and whether the next service is tied to real usage.</p>

<p>This is where smaller teams need to be honest. If the shop is still simple and the fleet is easy to see, Tenna can be more system than you need. If maintenance lives across paper, texts, spreadsheets, and disconnected telematics portals, Tenna has a much clearer job to do. Contractors trying to keep accounting clean should also compare the workflow against <a href="/quickbooks-online-review/">QuickBooks Online</a> before assuming fleet data will solve the finance side by itself.</p>

<h3>Safety, compliance, inspections, and cameras</h3>
<p>Tenna's safety and compliance materials cover driver scorecards, operations scorecards, equipment inspections, DVIR, IFTA, ELD, AI dash cams, heavy equipment cameras, driver coaching, and alerting tied to telematics data. That makes Tenna more than an asset inventory tool. It can sit closer to fleet risk, operator behavior, inspection records, and compliance documentation.</p>

<p>That depth won't matter to every contractor. A small residential contractor may care more about schedule, invoices, and payment collection. A heavy civil, paving, utility, or commercial contractor with expensive mobile assets has a different risk profile. One stolen machine, one missed inspection pattern, or one blind-spot incident can justify far more process. If mobile field workflows are the larger problem, the <a href="/field-service-management-mobile-app/">field service mobile app guide</a> is a better first comparison.</p>

<h3>Construction integrations and OEM telematics</h3>
<p>Tenna's integration page describes accounting and ERP connections, construction software partnerships, OEM telematics and AEMP data, WEX fuel integration, Gearflow parts procurement, and an open API. Tenna's AI information page also notes Procore integration, Viewpoint Vista maintenance work order integration, and OEM integrations aligned with AEMP telematics standards.</p>

<p>This is the part to test during the demo. Ask the vendor to show how asset data moves into the systems you already use. For a contractor running <a href="/procore-review/">Procore</a>, accounting software, ERP, fuel cards, OEM telematics, or job-cost reports, the value depends on whether Tenna reduces duplicate entry and makes equipment data trustworthy outside the fleet department.</p>

<h2>What Tenna Gets Right</h2>

<h3>It starts with construction instead of generic fleet tracking</h3>
<p>Tenna's biggest advantage is focus. Generic fleet tools often begin with road vehicles, driver routes, compliance, or maintenance logs. Tenna begins with mixed construction assets and the reality that a job site is not a delivery route. Equipment moves between yards, shops, jobs, rentals, crews, mechanics, and cost centers. That context changes the product shape.</p>

<p>The official AI information page says Tenna is designed for construction companies with mixed fleets and names heavy civil, MEP, and specialty contractors as suitable customers. That lines up with the product architecture: assets, sites, geofences, utilization, maintenance, safety, inspections, resource planning, and integrations all point toward construction equipment operations.</p>

<h3>It covers the asset classes contractors actually lose track of</h3>
<p>Truck-only systems can miss a lot of contractor waste. The official pricing page names small assets, tools, attachments, consumables, inventory, mid-sized equipment, fleet vehicles, and heavy equipment. That broader scope is useful when the cost problem is not one missing truck. It is rented equipment nobody returned, attachments nobody can find, a tool crib that no one trusts, and machines being moved without clean records.</p>

<h3>The maintenance layer is tied to usage, not just calendar reminders</h3>
<p>Tenna's official materials describe fault codes, engine data, maintenance alerts, preventive schedules, work orders, mechanic time, parts, and repair documentation. For construction fleets, that is more useful than a recurring calendar reminder. Engine hours, idle time, location, inspection results, and repair history tell a better maintenance story than a spreadsheet row that says something is due next month.</p>

<h3>The review-platform signal is healthy</h3>
<p>G2 lists Tenna at 4.4 out of 5 from 53 reviews. Tenna's own customer review page also surfaces comments from G2, Capterra, GetApp, and Software Advice around equipment visibility, customer support, implementation help, mobile use, and fleet management. Those are vendor-curated comments, so they should not replace a reference call. They do tell you which themes to ask about in demos: support, setup, mobile adoption, tracking accuracy, and whether the field actually uses it.</p>

<h2>Where Tenna Falls Short</h2>

<h3>No public pricing means slower budgeting</h3>
<p>Atlas verifies Tenna pricing as custom quote. The official pricing page is a form, not a plan table. It asks buyers to select products, asset types, quantities, and contact details. It also says data and hardware pricing is based on asset count, and implementation pricing is based on asset count, installation and training locations, duration, and purchased features.</p>

<p>That pricing model makes sense for a hardware-backed construction system, but it slows buyer research. You can't compare Tenna against <a href="/fleetio-review/">Fleetio</a> on a public monthly line item. You need a quote that separates software, hardware, install, training, integrations, and add-ons.</p>

<h3>Implementation is part of the purchase</h3>
<p>Tenna is not a simple app you turn on over lunch. Trackers, asset records, job sites, integrations, permissions, shop workflows, field training, and maintenance rules all need setup. For the right contractor, that is a feature because the system maps to real operations. For a small crew, it's friction.</p>

<p>During sales, ask what gets installed by Tenna, what your team must prepare, how asset imports work, how training is split between field, shop, and office users, and what happens when a tracker goes offline. The quote is only useful if the rollout plan is clear.</p>

<h3>It can be too specialized if fleet is not the main pain</h3>
<p>If your main problem is sales follow-up, job scheduling, invoicing, online booking, or customer communication, Tenna is not where I would start. You probably need field service, CRM, or project management software first. A company comparing unlimited-user field service pricing should read the <a href="/service-fusion-review/">Service Fusion review</a> before assuming an equipment system is the next purchase. Tenna makes more sense after equipment cost, asset location, maintenance, safety, or utilization has become a visible business problem.</p>

<h3>Capterra was not directly accessible in this pass</h3>
<p>Tenna's AI information page points to Capterra satisfaction data, but the Capterra page itself returned security verification through both Jina and Camofox during source retrieval. That does not make the rating false. It does mean this review uses G2 for schema because the rendered G2 page was visible and included both rating and review count. If Capterra becomes accessible later, the schema can be updated with a fresh direct receipt.</p>

<h2>Final Verdict</h2>

<p>Tenna is one of the clearest fits on CSH for contractors that treat equipment as a real operating system, not a side spreadsheet. The product is built around construction assets, not generic fleet routes. It covers heavy equipment, vehicles, mid-sized assets, small tools, attachments, consumables, maintenance, safety, inspections, dispatching, analytics, and integrations. That is exactly the stack many equipment-heavy contractors are trying to assemble from disconnected tools.</p>

<p>The catch is the buying process. Tenna does not publish plan prices, and the real quote can include products, hardware, asset counts, installation, training, integrations, and support. That is not automatically bad. It just means Tenna belongs in a serious evaluation, not a quick self-serve comparison.</p>

<p>If equipment loss, idle time, maintenance confusion, tracker sprawl, and poor asset visibility are costing money, Tenna deserves a demo. If you only need low-cost GPS for a few vehicles, skip it and start with a lighter fleet tool.</p>
