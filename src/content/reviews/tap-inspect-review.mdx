<p>Tap Inspect is home inspection software for inspectors who want the report finished before the job follows them home. The product is built around iPhone and iPad field reporting, with Autopilot handling the office chores around the inspection: agreements, invoices, reminders, client messaging, and payment collection.</p>

<p>The reason to look at Tap Inspect is focus. It's not trying to be a broad contractor CRM or a construction project system. It's for home inspectors who need to schedule the job, collect observations and photos, build a clear report, share results through a client portal, and get paid. If that's the job, the pricing is easy to model: Pay as You Go at $7.50 per job sold in blocks of 20 for $150, Unlimited at $90/mo, or Inspection Team at $90/mo plus $45 per inspector/user per month.</p>

<h3>Right for you if:</h3>
<ul>
<li>You inspect homes from an iPhone or iPad and want to complete more of the report on site</li>

<li>You need agreements, invoices, reminders, payments, and report delivery tied to each job</li>

<h2>Feature Deep Dive</h2>

<h3>iPhone and iPad field report writing</h3>
<p>Tap Inspect's core bet is simple: the inspection report should be built while the inspector is still at the property. The official home page says inspectors can schedule, inspect, and share results from the field through the iPhone and iPad app. The how-it-works page describes recording observations, using a flexible inspection checklist, adding photos, reusing comments, reviewing the report, creating a report summary, and sharing results from the mobile app.</p>

<p>That makes Tap Inspect a good fit for inspectors who already trust Apple devices on site. The field workflow matters because report delay eats into nights and weekends. If photos, notes, limitations, and summary items are captured in the same mobile workflow, the report has a better chance of leaving with the client and agent before the inspector starts the next job.</p>

<h3>Autopilot for booking chores</h3>
<p>Tap Inspect uses Autopilot as the back-office layer around the report writer. Official pages describe scheduling new jobs, open appointment slots, personal calendar conflict checks, signed agreements, invoices, payment collection, email reminders, and text reminders. Those chores are small in isolation. Together, they are the admin drag that keeps a solo inspector from adding another inspection slot.</p>

<p>The important test isn't whether Autopilot looks polished in a demo. Build a real inspection job during the free start. Add a client, add an agent, send an agreement, trigger a reminder, send an invoice, and confirm what the client sees before report delivery. If that path works cleanly, Tap Inspect is doing the work it was built to do.</p>

<h3>Client portal and report delivery</h3>
<p>The how-it-works page describes a client portal, custom messaging to clients and agents, and the ability to require signature and payment before results are viewed. That is a practical inspection workflow. The report is the product, but the delivery experience shapes how professional the inspector looks to the buyer and agent.</p>

<p>Client portal delivery also helps with recordkeeping. If the report, agreement, invoice, payment status, and shared result are tied to the same job, the office has fewer loose ends after closing. This matters most for inspectors who get follow-up questions weeks later and need to recover what was sent, when it was sent, and who had access.</p>

<h3>Flexible pricing by inspection volume</h3>
<p>Atlas and the official pricing page match. Tap Inspect publishes Pay as You Go at $7.50 per job, sold in blocks of 20 for $150. Unlimited is $90/mo. Inspection Team is $90/mo plus $45 per inspector/user per month. The official pages also promote a free start for the first 5 home inspections.</p>

<p>The pricing shape is the best part of the buying path. A new inspector can buy a report block instead of taking on a monthly bill. A full-time solo inspector can move to Unlimited when volume supports it. A small firm can price the Team plan before talking to sales. The catch is volume math. Once you're publishing enough reports each month, Pay as You Go stops being the cheap path.</p>

<h2>What Tap Inspect Gets Right</h2>

<h3>The product knows its lane</h3>
<p>Tap Inspect doesn't ask a home inspector to buy a generic contractor system. It focuses on the inspection job: report writing, observations, photos, scheduling, agreements, reminders, payments, and client delivery. That restraint is useful. Inspectors don't need roofing production boards or broad field service dispatch if the bottleneck is report turnaround.</p>

<p>If you're comparing broader contractor tools, use the <a href="/best-home-inspection-software/">home inspection software roundup</a> first. Tap Inspect belongs in the Apple-based, report-first lane. Spectora, Home Inspector Pro, HomeGauge, and Palmtech sit in adjacent lanes with different device and office assumptions.</p>

<h3>Low-volume inspectors get a real entry path</h3>
<p>Pay as You Go is the obvious hook for new, part-time, and seasonal inspectors. At $7.50 per job and $150 for a 20-report block, Tap Inspect lets a buyer test the workflow without committing to a monthly plan. That's helpful when inspection volume is still uneven.</p>

<p>The free start for the first 5 home inspections gives an even cleaner test. Don't waste it on sample data. Build real reports, attach photos, send a client portal link, process an agreement, and check how long it takes to get from driveway to delivery.</p>

<h3>The mobile proof is better than the category average</h3>
<p>The Apple App Store page showed 4.8 out of 5 from 4.9K ratings. For a product that depends heavily on field use, that's the right place to look. A web dashboard can impress the owner in a demo and still fail when an inspector is walking a crawlspace, shooting photos, and trying to finish notes on site.</p>

<p>That rating shouldn't be treated as a blank check. It's Apple-only evidence. If your team uses Android, this proof doesn't answer the device question. If your team uses iPad and iPhone on every job, it's a meaningful signal.</p>

<h2>Where Tap Inspect Falls Short</h2>

<h3>The Apple focus narrows the buyer pool</h3>
<p>Tap Inspect's public positioning is built around iPhone and iPad. That is a strength for Apple-based shops and a hard stop for Android-first teams. If your inspectors already carry Android phones or tablets, do not force a device switch just because the report writer looks attractive.</p>

<p>Device habits are sticky. Inspectors need the app to work under pressure, with photos, defects, client questions, and schedule changes happening at once. If the device fit is wrong, the software fit is wrong.</p>

<h3>High-volume teams need to check the team math</h3>
<p>Pay as You Go looks clean at low volume. Unlimited at $90/mo is straightforward for a full-time solo inspector. The Team plan is where buyers need to slow down: $90/mo plus $45 per inspector/user per month. That's still visible pricing, which is good, but it means each extra inspector changes the monthly budget.</p>

<p>A three-inspector shop should model the base plan plus the added inspector/user charges before comparing Tap Inspect with Spectora, Home Inspector Pro, HomeGauge, or Palmtech. The right answer depends on report volume, device standards, office work, and how much team management the company needs.</p>

<h3>It is not a broad contractor management system</h3>
<p>Tap Inspect isn't trying to manage general contracting projects, estimates, production crews, inventory, or job costing. That's fine for home inspectors. It's a mismatch for a contractor trying to turn an inspection app into a full operating system.</p>

<p>If your inspection company also runs repair work, remodeling, or field service crews, keep those workflows separate in the buying process. Tap Inspect can handle inspection reporting and related client handoff. It shouldn't be judged as if it were a full construction management platform.</p>
