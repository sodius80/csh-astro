<p>FieldEdge is aimed at the contractor who has outgrown entry-level field service software but does not want the cost or implementation burden that usually comes with ServiceTitan. It is strongest where HVAC, plumbing, and electrical shops care most: QuickBooks sync, flat-rate pricing, and dispatching. The tradeoff is weak price transparency, a binding contract model, and a mobile app reputation that is hard to ignore.</p>
<div style="background:#f4f5f7;border-left:4px solid #1C2B4B;padding:16px 20px;margin:24px 0;border-radius:0 8px 8px 0;">
<p style="margin:0 0 8px 0;"><strong style="color:#1a7a3f;">Right for:</strong> Established HVAC, plumbing, and electrical shops that already run on QuickBooks, have office staff handling dispatch, and want a built-in flat-rate pricebook for techs in the field.</p>
<p style="margin:0;"><strong style="color:#c0392b;">Not for:</strong> Solo operators, very small crews, or any company that depends heavily on a polished field mobile app and wants clear month-to-month pricing before talking to sales.</p>

<h2>What FieldEdge Gets Right</h2>
<p><strong>QuickBooks integration is the real draw.</strong> This is the feature that comes up most often in positive reviews and in FieldEdge’s own positioning. The sync goes beyond simple invoice export. Contractors describe it as a meaningful reduction in double entry because customer records, payments, and job data stay tighter to the accounting workflow than they do in many competing systems.</p>
<p><strong>The flat-rate pricebook is built for service trades.</strong> FieldEdge includes a parts and pricing structure that fits how HVAC, plumbing, and electrical contractors actually quote repair work. Techs can pull options in the field, build multi-option estimates, and work from a more standardized pricing system instead of winging it job by job.</p>
<p><strong>The dispatch board looks like software built for an office team, not just a solo owner.</strong> Skill-based assignment, scheduling, and customer history all show up as recurring strengths. For shops with a dispatcher and multiple trucks on the road, that operational layer matters more than glossy marketing screenshots.</p>
<p><strong>The feature set is deeper than entry-level platforms.</strong> Reporting, service agreements, pricebook structure, and field quoting are more mature than what small crews usually get from simpler tools. If Housecall Pro feels too lightweight and ServiceTitan feels like too much, FieldEdge does occupy a legitimate middle ground.</p>
<h2>Where FieldEdge Falls Short</h2>
<p><strong>The mobile app is the biggest concern.</strong> The pattern in reviews is too consistent to dismiss as a few angry customers. Low app-store scores, complaints about glitches, and reports of time-tracking or field usability issues all point in the same direction: if your technicians will live in the app all day, you need to test that experience hard before signing anything.</p>
<p><strong>The sales and implementation experience gets mixed feedback.</strong> Multiple reviewers describe being told the software could handle workflows that later proved rough or incomplete. Others describe long implementations and painful launch issues. That does not mean every deployment goes badly, but it does mean you should get critical promises in writing instead of relying on the demo.</p>
<p><strong>Contract structure raises the stakes.</strong> No free trial, quote-only pricing, and recurring mentions of auto-renewal make this a higher-commitment buy than Jobber or Housecall Pro. If you realize two months in that the app frustrates your team, you may not have an easy exit.</p>
<p><strong>Add-ons and changing costs muddy the real total.</strong> FieldEdge publishes plan names and feature grids, but not public pricing. Reviewers also mention rising costs and previously included capabilities shifting into add-ons. That makes the all-in number less predictable than it should be.</p>
<h2>Pricing Breakdown</h2>
<p>FieldEdge does <strong>not</strong> publish plan pricing publicly as of March 2026. You have to go through a sales process to get a quote. That alone puts it at a disadvantage for small contractors trying to compare options cleanly.</p>
<p>The company currently markets three core plans: <strong>Select</strong>, <strong>Premier</strong>, and <strong>Elite</strong>. Its public pricing/features page confirms a few important structural details: Select includes 2 mobile licenses, Premier includes 4, and Elite includes 6. Premier adds multi-option quotes and more advanced service agreement/reporting features. Elite adds unlimited saved reports plus extras like outbound call recording and additional growth tools.</p>
<p>For the actual dollars, the most consistent numbers in user reports and third-party pricing research are roughly <strong>$100/month per office user</strong> and <strong>$125/month per technician</strong>, plus a one-time setup fee that often lands somewhere around <strong>$500 to $2,000</strong>. Treat those as directional, not official. FieldEdge does not publish them, and your quote may differ.</p>
<p>That means a 5-tech shop with 2 office staff could easily land around <strong>$825/month before onboarding or add-ons</strong>, based on user-reported pricing patterns. If QuickBooks sync, texting, proposal features, or other extras are essential to your workflow, ask whether they are included in your quoted tier or priced separately.</p>
<p><em>Bottom line on pricing:</em> the product may still make financial sense for the right HVAC or plumbing business, but the lack of transparency means you should budget cautiously and verify the renewal terms before signing.</p>
<h2>What Users Actually Say</h2>
<p><strong>QuickBooks and back-office efficiency (positive reviewers):</strong> Contractors who like FieldEdge usually point here first. The common theme is that the accounting connection saves time and removes cleanup work between the field system and the office. For businesses already committed to QuickBooks, that benefit appears to be real.</p>
<p><strong>Mobile frustration and reliability issues (critical reviewers):</strong> Negative reviews cluster around the field experience. One owner described the app and integration issues as so disruptive that the software felt more like a burden than a time-saver. Another long-term customer said technical issues were becoming more common across both office and technician devices. The wording changes from review to review, but the complaint pattern does not.</p>
<p><strong>Sales, contracts, and rising costs (mixed-to-negative reviewers):</strong> A separate theme is disappointment after the sale. Reviewers mention unclear expectations during the demo process, difficulty getting a true hands-on evaluation before signing, and frustration with auto-renewal or added costs later. If you move forward, this is the part to slow down and inspect carefully.</p>
<h2>Bottom Line</h2>
<p>FieldEdge is a credible option for established service-trade companies that need stronger QuickBooks integration, a structured flat-rate pricebook, and a real dispatch workflow. That is the case for it.</p>
<p>The case against it is just as important: weak pricing transparency, contract friction, and a mobile experience that too many reviewers describe as problematic. If your office runs the business and techs only touch the app for part of the day, FieldEdge may still be worth a serious demo. If your technicians live on their phones and tablets, I would be cautious.</p>

<h2>FAQ</h2>
<h3>Is FieldEdge worth it?</h3>
<p>For established HVAC, plumbing, or electrical shops that already run on QuickBooks and have office staff handling dispatch, FieldEdge can be worth it. The QuickBooks sync and flat-rate pricebook are genuinely useful for that kind of operation. But if your technicians depend heavily on the mobile app all day, the consistent complaints about app reliability are a real concern you should test before signing.</p>
<h3>How much does FieldEdge cost?</h3>
<p>FieldEdge does not publish pricing publicly. Based on user reports and third-party research, expect roughly $100/month per office user and $125/month per technician, plus a one-time setup fee between $500 and $2,000. A 5-tech shop with 2 office staff could land around $825/month before onboarding or add-ons. Get your specific quote in writing before committing.</p>
<h3>What are the biggest downsides of FieldEdge?</h3>
<p>Three things stand out. First, the mobile app gets consistently poor reviews for glitches and unreliable field performance. Second, pricing is quote-only with no free trial, so you are committing before you can fully test the product. Third, contract auto-renewal and rising costs catch some users off guard. If you move forward, get the renewal terms and included features confirmed in writing.</p>
<h3>Does FieldEdge work with QuickBooks?</h3>
<p>Yes, and this is the main reason contractors choose FieldEdge over competitors. The integration goes beyond basic invoice export — it syncs customer records, payments, and job data in a way that reduces double entry. Users consistently call it one of the strongest QuickBooks connections in the field service category.</p>
<h3>Is FieldEdge better than ServiceTitan?</h3>
<p>It depends on your size. FieldEdge is usually the better fit for 5-to-20 tech shops that run on QuickBooks and want a simpler implementation. ServiceTitan makes more sense for larger operations with 15+ techs that need deeper reporting, more integrations, and built-in financing. Neither offers a free trial, so compare carefully before committing to either.</p>
