## Where Exayard Falls Short

<p><strong>The credit model requires real-world testing.</strong> Starter includes $100 in AI credits/month per user and Professional includes $250 in AI credits/month per user. Those allowances are public. What is not public in the reviewed pages is the exact credit consumption pattern for different drawing types, overage pricing, and what happens when a team runs out of credits mid-month. A contractor bidding a few residential jobs may be fine. A heavy bid desk with many complex plan sets should not assume the subscription price is the final monthly cost.</p>

<p><strong>The 3-day trial is short for trust-building.</strong> A takeoff tool affects bid accuracy. Three days can prove whether the interface works, but it is not much time to validate accuracy across architectural, structural, MEP, landscape, and specialty drawings. The trial should be treated like a focused test sprint: upload real jobs, compare Exayard's output against your previous manual takeoff, and document where estimator review is still required.</p>

<p><strong>Offline and mobile behavior should be confirmed.</strong> The official pages reviewed for this audit did not publish a clear offline-mode promise. That is not a criticism by itself, but it is a buying question. If your estimators work from job sites, rural areas, airplanes, or construction trailers with unreliable internet, ask directly whether Exayard can be used without a live connection and what mobile or tablet workflows are supported.</p>

<p><strong>Named integrations are not the same as general export support.</strong> Exayard's public pages support Excel, CSV, and PDF export language. They also reference integrations at a higher level, especially for Enterprise. I would not assume a specific accounting, project-management, or CRM connection is included until sales confirms it in writing. For many contractors, exports may be enough. For larger teams, direct integration scope may decide whether Enterprise is worth the quote.</p>

## Feature Deep Dive

<p><strong>AI count and measurement:</strong> Exayard's core promise is helping contractors count and measure from digital plans faster. The public feature language covers automatic scale detection, auto count, area measurement, and linear measurement. That is especially useful for repetitive takeoffs: doors, fixtures, rooms, runs, square footage, linear footage, and other quantities that are easy to miss when an estimator is moving quickly.</p>

<p><strong>Natural-language takeoff requests:</strong> Exayard positions AI interaction as part of the workflow, not just a background automation layer. The practical buying test is whether your estimator can ask for the quantities that matter in your trade and then review the output quickly. AI-assisted takeoff is only useful if the estimator can see the assumptions, correct mistakes, and trust the final quantity list enough to bid with it.</p>

<p><strong>Estimate tables and templates:</strong> Starter includes basic estimate tables; Professional adds custom templates. This is where Exayard can save time beyond measurement. If you regularly price similar scopes, templates can reduce repetitive setup. During a trial, create a real template for one of your common jobs and see whether the quantities, line items, and export format match your existing estimating process.</p>

<p><strong>Exports:</strong> Excel, CSV, and PDF exports are important because they let Exayard fit into a contractor's existing system of record. A small remodeler may export a PDF for review and keep pricing in a spreadsheet. A larger specialty contractor may export CSV data into a more formal estimating or proposal tool. Before buying, test the export with your real line-item structure and confirm that cleanup time does not erase the takeoff speed gain.</p>

<p><strong>Enterprise integrations and support:</strong> Enterprise is the tier to evaluate if Exayard needs to connect to a broader tech stack or support a larger estimating department. The public page references custom integrations, dedicated account management, on-site training, an SLA guarantee, and volume discounts. Ask for specific integration commitments, implementation timelines, support response terms, and training deliverables before comparing the quote against more established estimating platforms.</p>

## Buying Checklist

<ul>
<li><strong>Upload real drawings:</strong> Use plan sets from jobs you already estimated manually so you can compare quantities.</li>
<li><strong>Test multiple scopes:</strong> Try one simple residential plan, one messy revision set, and one larger or more complex project.</li>
<li><strong>Track credit usage:</strong> Record how much AI credit each test consumes and ask what happens if credits run out.</li>
<li><strong>Review estimator control:</strong> Confirm how easy it is to correct AI mistakes, adjust scale, and override quantities.</li>
<li><strong>Export into your workflow:</strong> Send Excel, CSV, or PDF outputs into your current estimate/proposal process and measure cleanup time.</li>
<li><strong>Ask about integrations:</strong> If you need a specific accounting, CRM, or project-management system, confirm whether the connection is native, custom, Enterprise-only, or not available.</li>
<li><strong>Confirm offline and mobile access:</strong> Do not assume job-site use works without internet unless Exayard confirms it.</li>
<li><strong>Clarify storage and retention:</strong> Ask how long projects and plan sets are stored, whether there are project limits, and how data export works if you cancel.</li>
</ul>

## Implementation Notes

<p>Do not roll out Exayard by asking the whole estimating team to “try AI” on live bids with no measurement standard. Start with a controlled comparison. Pick two or three completed jobs where you still have the plan set, the original takeoff, and the winning or final estimate. Run those drawings through Exayard, export the quantities, and compare them against the quantities your team already trusted. The goal is not to prove that AI is perfect. The goal is to understand where it saves time, where estimator review is still mandatory, and which plan types are too messy for automation.</p>

<p>Set up a simple accuracy log during the trial. Track drawing type, number of sheets, trade scope, AI credits consumed, manual cleanup time, export format, and whether the estimator would use the result on a real bid. That log will answer the buying question better than a demo. A tool that saves an hour on clean residential plans but creates twenty minutes of cleanup on every revision set may still be valuable, but the value depends on your actual bid mix.</p>

<p>Professional buyers should also test templates early. If your estimating process relies on standard assemblies, preferred suppliers, margin rules, or scope-specific proposal language, a blank takeoff export is only half the workflow. Build one real template and see whether Exayard's estimate table structure maps cleanly to your bid format. If the template reduces repetitive setup, the $249/user/month Professional plan may be easier to justify. If the team still rebuilds every estimate manually after export, Exayard is mainly a measurement accelerator rather than an estimating system.</p>

<p>Finally, decide who owns quality control. AI takeoff output should not bypass estimator review. Assign one person to verify scale, quantities, symbols, exclusions, and assumptions before a number reaches the customer. Exayard can speed up the front end of the estimate, but the contractor is still responsible for the bid. That operating discipline is what separates useful AI assistance from risky automation theater.</p>
