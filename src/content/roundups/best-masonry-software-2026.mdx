<p>Masonry software should be judged by the places where masonry contractors lose money: a wall condition missed during takeoff, a bond pattern handled like a generic count, mortar or grout assumptions buried in a spreadsheet, production rates copied from the wrong job, or a change order written after the crew already did the work. The right tool is not always the biggest construction platform. It is the tool that makes the estimate, plan record, field notes, and job-cost story more reliable.</p>

<p>For masonry contractors, the buying decision usually splits into two tracks. One track is estimating accuracy: quantities, bond patterns, openings, lintels, reinforcing, mortar, grout, waste, scaffolding, equipment, and crew production rates. The other track is company management: schedules, daily logs, time cards, documents, photos, invoices, QuickBooks, job costing, and field communication. This roundup separates those jobs so you do not buy a full ERP when you only need better takeoff, or buy a PDF tool when the office needs job costing.</p>

<p><strong>Not for:</strong> contractors that only need more leads, owners who still price every simple job accurately from a short template, or companies expecting software to fix margin before they have agreed on assemblies, cost codes, labor rates, waste factors, markup, exclusions, and change order responsibility.</p>

## How to Choose Masonry Software

<p>Start by naming the problem. If estimates are late or inconsistent, the first demo should focus on masonry estimating and takeoff. If jobs are sold correctly but the office loses track of schedules, time cards, photos, invoices, and QuickBooks entries, the first demo should be a construction management platform. If the company is larger and accounting is the center of the problem, an ERP conversation may make sense. Buying from a feature checklist usually leads to paying for functions the team will not use.</p>

<p>For masonry-specific estimating, Masonry Estimating Made Easy is the most direct fit here. Its current public page lists a $399/month subscription and positions the product around masonry bid preparation, unlimited do-it-yourself estimates, and official-site claims of up to 75% faster bid creation and $100M+ in contracts won using its estimation engine. Those claims should be treated as vendor claims, not independent proof. The demo still needs to prove your actual wall assemblies, bond patterns, mortar, grout, reinforcing, crew rates, alternates, exclusions, exports, and bid review process.</p>

<p>For broad construction management, Contractor Foreman is the value option. It starts at $49/month on annual billing for 1 user, but most masonry companies should compare the higher tiers because daily logs, time cards, job-costing reports, QuickBooks workflow, takeoffs, and client portal are tier-dependent. A lower starting price is useful only if the plan includes the workflow that is failing. If the team needs eight field and office users, compare the Plus tier, not only Basic.</p>

<p>Buildxact belongs in the conversation when masonry is one part of residential building or remodeling. Foundation includes digital takeoffs and estimate costings, while Pro adds job management, schedules, and mobile access. That is useful for builders handling foundations, veneers, patios, additions, framing, finishes, and trade coordination. It is less compelling for a masonry-only subcontractor that mainly needs trade-specific production math and bid control.</p>

<p>Jonas Construction Software and Bluebeam Revu sit at opposite ends of the decision. Jonas is for larger contractors that need ERP, accounting, payroll, job costing, operational modules, documents, and estimating or takeoff tied into the back office. Bluebeam is for plan markup and quantity work. It can support a strong estimating process, but it is not a business management system. A masonry company may use Bluebeam beside Contractor Foreman, Buildxact, Jonas, or a dedicated estimating process.</p>

<p>Finally, compare total cost instead of starting price. Include users, billing term, training, implementation, data import, support, add-ons, takeoff seats, viewer seats, mobile access, QuickBooks or accounting integration, storage, renewal terms, cancellation, and export rights. The least expensive tool can become costly if it requires double entry or if field users avoid it because setup was rushed.</p>

## Do You Need This Yet?

<p>Masonry software becomes worth paying for when the job record no longer stays accurate in simple tools. A small masonry contractor can run lean with a spreadsheet, PDF markup, a shared folder, and accounting software while the owner personally checks every bid and job. That does not mean software is useless. It means the buying trigger should be a real failure point, not the feeling that every competitor has a platform.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>You do not need it yet</strong> if one person can still measure plans, price material, apply labor production rates, schedule crews, approve changes, invoice, and reconcile job costs without losing details.</li>
<li><strong>You need it now</strong> if estimators are rebuilding takeoffs from old PDFs, crews are working from stale plan notes, change orders are late, or the office cannot tell whether a brick, block, stone, or veneer job made money until weeks after closeout.</li>
</ul>

<p>The middle stage is common. A company may not need Jonas, but may need Contractor Foreman because several people need one place for schedules, time cards, documents, and job-cost reports. Another company may not need a management suite, but may need Bluebeam because plan measurement and revision control are the daily bottleneck. A masonry-heavy bidder may need Masonry Estimating Made Easy because generic estimate templates do not reflect how the crew actually works.</p>

<p>Before signing anything, write the current failure point in one sentence. Examples: wall takeoffs are inconsistent, mortar and grout assumptions are hidden, field changes are not priced, job costs arrive too late, or crews do not see updated plans. Then make every demo prove that exact issue using a real masonry project, not a clean sample file.</p>

## Masonry Software Buying Checklist

<p>Use real masonry jobs in the demo. A sample project with clean walls and no revisions will not prove whether the system can handle your messy bid. Bring at least one job that won, one job that lost money, one job with plan revisions, and one job where the office had to rebuild field history from emails, photos, or marked-up plans.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Test the takeoff.</strong> Measure wall types, openings, returns, corners, lintels, control joints, veneer, backup walls, foundations, caps, and alternates from a real plan set.</li>
<li><strong>Test masonry assumptions.</strong> Add bond patterns, mortar, grout, reinforcing, flashing, weeps, anchors, waste, scaffolding, equipment, mobilization, cleanup, taxes, overhead, and markup.</li>
<li><strong>Test labor production.</strong> Compare crew rates by wall type, height, access, lift requirement, weather exposure, supervision, overtime, and jobsite conditions.</li>
<li><strong>Test revisions.</strong> Upload a revised plan, identify what changed, update quantities, preserve the old version, and show how the change affects proposal and job cost.</li>
<li><strong>Test field records.</strong> Capture daily notes, photos, time cards, delays, material issues, safety notes, inspection items, and change requests from a phone.</li>
<li><strong>Test accounting handoff.</strong> Confirm how estimates, budgets, cost codes, invoices, payments, purchase orders, payroll, and QuickBooks or ERP entries move without duplicate entry.</li>
<li><strong>Test exit risk.</strong> Ask how to export customers, estimates, assemblies, price data, takeoffs, photos, documents, schedules, invoices, job costs, and project history if you leave.</li>
</ul>

<p>Also decide who owns implementation. Masonry software fails when the estimator assumes the office will build cost codes, the office assumes the foreman will enter daily reports, and the foreman assumes the PM will clean it up later. Assign one internal owner for assemblies, production rates, templates, permissions, field rules, accounting handoff, training, and quality control.</p>

## FAQ

### What is the best masonry software for most contractors?

<p>Masonry Estimating Made Easy is the best first demo when the main problem is masonry-specific estimating for brick, block, stone, veneer, or commercial masonry scopes. Contractor Foreman is better when the company needs lower-cost project management. Buildxact fits residential builders with masonry as one trade. Jonas fits larger ERP and accounting needs. Bluebeam Revu fits PDF takeoff and plan markup.</p>

### How much should a masonry contractor budget for software?

<p>Budget depends on the workflow. Masonry Estimating Made Easy lists $399/month. Contractor Foreman starts at $49/month on annual billing, with higher annual tiers up to Unlimited at $332/month. Buildxact Foundation is $199/month monthly or $169/month annually, with Pro and Master higher. Bluebeam Revu starts at $260/user/year. Jonas Construction Software requires a custom quote.</p>

### Do I need masonry-specific estimating software or general construction software?

<p>Choose masonry-specific estimating software if the problem is bid accuracy, bond patterns, mortar, grout, reinforcing, wall conditions, waste, crew production rates, and takeoff consistency. Choose general construction software if the problem is scheduling, daily logs, time cards, documents, invoicing, QuickBooks, field notes, and job-cost reporting after the job is sold. Some contractors need both.</p>

### Is Masonry Estimating Made Easy worth $399/month?

<p>It can be worth a demo if the company regularly bids masonry work where estimate time and missed quantities affect profit. The official site claims up to 75% faster bid prep and $100M+ in contracts won using its estimation engine, but those are vendor claims. Test the product with a real bid and confirm current launch status, support, exports, and cancellation terms before relying on it.</p>

### Is Contractor Foreman enough for masonry contractors?

<p>Contractor Foreman can be enough when the company needs broad construction management at a lower published price. It can help with schedules, logs, time cards, documents, reports, estimates, and QuickBooks by tier. It is not enough by itself if the primary issue is detailed masonry estimate logic. In that case, pair it with a stronger takeoff or masonry estimating process.</p>

### Should masonry contractors use Bluebeam Revu?

<p>Bluebeam Revu is useful for PDF measurement, markup, overlays, plan review, and quantity coordination. It is a good fit when estimators already have a method for turning quantities into labor, material, equipment, markup, and proposal language. It is not a full masonry business platform because it does not replace scheduling, invoicing, payroll, accounting, or job-cost reporting.</p>

### What is the biggest mistake when buying masonry software?

<p>The biggest mistake is buying from a generic feature list instead of testing a real masonry job. The demo should prove takeoff, bond patterns, wall types, mortar, grout, reinforcing, waste, labor production rates, revisions, change orders, field documentation, accounting handoff, and export. If the process still depends on hidden spreadsheets and memory, the software has not solved the main problem.</p>
