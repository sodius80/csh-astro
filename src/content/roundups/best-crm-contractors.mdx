## Do You Need This Yet?

CRM software pays for itself when quote volume gets high enough that memory stops being a system. Two quick checks:

- **You don't need it yet** if you send a handful of quotes a month and can remember every callback without a list. Your phone's notes app still works at that scale.
- **You need it now** if jobs have slipped away because nobody followed up, or you cannot tell which lead source produces revenue.

If you're in the middle - growing, but not buried in leads - Jobber is the safer first test. The built-in quote follow-up automation on Connect and higher plans cuts the risk that unanswered quotes disappear into a notebook or inbox while you figure out what else you need.

## Product Reviews

### 1. Jobber - Best All-in-One (CRM + Field Service)

<p>Jobber treats CRM as part of the field service job flow, not a separate sales database. Every client has a profile with past jobs, quotes, invoices, notes, and communication history. The practical benefit is simple: open quotes, quote status, follow-up, and dispatch live in the same place.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> Quote follow-up is the real CRM value. Jobber's help center says quote follow-ups can send email or text reminders for quotes that are still awaiting response, with up to two reminders on Connect and higher plans.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Jobber's separate Pipeline tool is included on Plus. Lower tiers lean more on quote and request tracking, so they are not a full sales-board setup. If you want lead-source reporting tied to campaign performance, compare Housecall Pro, ServiceTitan, or a standalone CRM.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Core is $29/mo when billed annually or $49 month-to-month for one user. Connect starts at $99/mo annual for one user or $149/mo annual for the team version, Grow starts at $149/mo annual for one user or $299/mo annual for the team version, and Plus starts at $529/mo annual with 15 users included. A 14-day free trial is available with no credit card required.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Service contractors who want CRM functionality built into their existing scheduling and invoicing workflow without managing a separate system.</p>

### 2. Housecall Pro - Best for Lead Follow-Up in Residential

<p>Housecall Pro puts more of the lead process on the surface. Its published Pipeline feature gives teams a visual place for opportunities, lead intake, mobile updates, and automated follow-ups. Its help center also documents lead-source tracking on customers, jobs, and estimates. For a residential service contractor with heavy inbound lead volume, that is a clearer CRM layer than Jobber's lower-tier quote tracking.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> Lead-source tracking. Housecall Pro lets teams assign lead sources to customers, jobs, and estimates, and its mobile leads workflow tracks open, won, and lost leads.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> The plan jumps are real. Contractors needing deeper marketing, follow-up, or reporting features may need Essentials at $149/mo annual or MAX at $299/mo annual.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Basic $59/mo annual or $79 monthly. Essentials $149/mo annual or $189 monthly. MAX $299/mo annual or $329 monthly, with additional users listed at $35/mo.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Residential service contractors with significant inbound lead volume who want pipeline visibility and lead source attribution.</p>

### 3. Pipeline CRM - Best Standalone CRM

<p>Pipeline CRM (formerly PipelineDeals) is a dedicated sales CRM that some contractors use alongside field service software when built-in CRM features like Jobber's are not deep enough. It is built for deal pipelines, contact histories, and sales activity tracking that goes beyond what a field service tool usually covers.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> Depth for complex sales pipelines. If a dedicated sales rep owns the pipeline while field operations run elsewhere, keeping those systems separate can make sense.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> The handoff is the main issue. Pipeline CRM doesn't connect natively to most field service tools, so you'd be running two systems and duplicating some data.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Start is $25/user/mo with annual billing or $29/user/mo monthly. Higher Pipeline CRM tiers scale per user; confirm Develop/Grow pricing against the current pricing page before signing.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors with dedicated sales staff managing a complex B2B pipeline who need more CRM depth than field service tools provide.</p>

For a contractor who wants something more structured than a spreadsheet but is not ready to pay yet, it works.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> The free tier gives small teams a no-card way to put leads in a structured system before paying for a platform.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> HubSpot is separate from field service. You'd manage leads in HubSpot and jobs in another tool, which creates data silos. The free tier has limits that can push teams toward paid tiers quickly.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Free CRM tools are available. HubSpot Starter seats now publish around $7/mo per seat for many hubs, while Professional pricing varies by hub and seat count.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who want to start managing leads in a structured system at no cost and aren't ready to commit to an all-in-one platform yet.</p>

### 5. Keap - Best for Marketing + CRM Together

<p>Keap (formerly Infusionsoft) is for small businesses that want CRM and email marketing without connecting two separate tools. For contractors, that means lead capture, automated follow-ups, and promotional emails live in one place instead of spreading contacts across Mailchimp, a spreadsheet, and a separate CRM.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> The automation. Keap can send a welcome sequence when someone fills out a contact form, tag them by service interest, and alert the office when they open an email or visit a pricing page. Most contractor-focused tools do not do that automatically.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Keap is not field service software. You still need Jobber, Housecall Pro, or similar tools for scheduling, dispatching, and invoices. The setup also takes longer than a simple CRM because there are more features to work through.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Keap starts at $299/mo billed monthly for 2 users and 1,500 contacts for the full platform. Text marketing Tier 1 is included, with higher text tiers from $24/mo to $279/mo and optional SMS/local-number add-ons. A free trial is available.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who want CRM and email marketing in one platform without stitching separate tools together.</p>

### 6. ServiceTitan - Best for Complex Operations

<p>ServiceTitan's CRM value shows up in the front office: campaign ROI scorecards, unique phone numbers tied to booked revenue, call booking workflows, customer communication, and marketing automation add-ons.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> Marketing attribution tied to booked revenue. For a larger service operation where campaign ROI matters, ServiceTitan has deeper revenue-attribution tools than small-business CRMs.</p>

## Pricing Reality for Contractor CRM Buyers

<p>CRM pricing looks simple until the contractor asks where sales work ends and field work begins. Jobber and Housecall Pro bundle CRM features into a broader field service workflow. For a small service company, that usually beats paying for a separate CRM and then copying job details into scheduling software. Jobber starts at $29/month on annual billing, while Housecall Pro starts at $59/month on annual billing.</p>

<p>Pipeline CRM and HubSpot make more sense when sales can live outside field service. Pipeline CRM starts at $25/user/month on annual billing and is built for a dedicated sales pipeline. HubSpot has a free CRM for up to two users, then paid Sales Hub seats start around $7/month per seat on annual billing. Those tools can fit contractors with commercial sales, repeat account management, or separate business development roles. They are less useful if the office needs one system from estimate to dispatch.</p>

<p>FollowUp CRM's pricing is quote-based across all three tiers (Essentials, Professional, Enterprise) with no published per-user rates. A 30-day money-back guarantee replaces the traditional free trial. Builder Prime uses request-based pricing for both Growth and Multiply plans, with published add-on rates for SMS and e-signatures. TopBuilder's pricing is request-only, with third-party estimates of $35-90/user/month.</p>

<p>ServiceTitan should not be treated as a cheap CRM upgrade. Its pricing is quote-based and tied to per-technician assumptions, package selection, implementation, and a larger operating platform. It belongs in the CRM shortlist when campaign tracking, call booking, customer history, and booked-revenue reporting are management-level problems, not when a two-tech shop simply needs quote reminders.</p>

## CRM Demo Checklist for Contractors

<p>Run the CRM demo with one real lead source. Ask the vendor to create a new lead from a phone call, website form, referral, or paid ad. Then have them assign an owner, send a quote, schedule follow-up, mark the quote won or lost, and show where the revenue source appears in reporting.</p>

<p>For Jobber and Housecall Pro, watch the handoff. When a quote is accepted, does the job move naturally into scheduling, customer communication, invoice, and payment collection? If a contractor still needs to retype the same information into another tool, the CRM benefit is smaller than the demo makes it look.</p>

<p>For Pipeline CRM and HubSpot, test sales discipline. Can the team build stages that match the real sales process? Can users see stale deals, overdue tasks, next follow-up date, lead source, and expected value without building custom reports from scratch? If the sales manager cannot review the pipeline in 15 minutes on Monday morning, the CRM will not change behavior.</p>

## Where Each CRM Is a Wrong Fit

<p><strong>Jobber</strong> is the wrong fit if the company needs a complex standalone sales pipeline with account management, commercial deal stages, and sales-only reporting. It works best when CRM sits inside field service operations.</p>

<p><strong>Housecall Pro</strong> is the wrong fit if the buyer only wants a cheap contact database. Its value shows up when pipeline, online booking, reminders, payments, reviews, and homeowner communication all matter.</p>

<p><strong>Pipeline CRM</strong> is the wrong fit if the office wants one system for dispatch and invoicing. It is a sales CRM, so the contractor still needs a clear handoff into field operations.</p>

<p><strong>HubSpot Free</strong> is the wrong fit if the contractor expects it to manage crews, appointments, invoices, or job costing. It is a good free CRM starting point, not field service software.</p>

<p><strong>Keap</strong> is the wrong fit if you only need a simple contact list or your operation runs entirely through field service software. The extra automation features add complexity that a basic CRM user won't need.</p>

<p><strong>ServiceTitan</strong> is the wrong fit for small shops that need reminders and a simple pipeline. It is built for larger field service operations where marketing attribution and booked revenue justify the implementation effort.</p>

<p><strong>FollowUp CRM</strong> is the wrong fit if your team needs field production tools, native mobile apps, or published pricing before talking to sales. It is a pre-construction sales CRM, not an all-in-one platform.</p>

<p><strong>Builder Prime</strong> is the wrong fit for general contractors outside home improvement, solo operators who need a lighter tool, or anyone who requires transparent subscription pricing before a demo conversation.</p>

<p><strong>TopBuilder</strong> is the wrong fit for small residential contractors under five people, teams that want an all-in-one platform with project management, or buyers who rely on abundant independent reviews before committing.</p>

## Implementation Plan for Better Follow-Up

<p>The first CRM project should be smaller than most buyers expect. Do not start with every feature. Start with the follow-up rule: every quote needs an owner, a status, a next action, and a due date. If those four fields are maintained, the CRM will prevent most lost follow-up before any advanced automation is added.</p>

<p>Week one should define stages such as new lead, appointment scheduled, quote sent, follow-up due, won, lost, and dormant. Week two should connect quote templates, customer communication, and basic reporting. Week three should review every lead older than seven days. Week four should compare lead source to booked work and decide which reports the owner will use.</p>

<p>Duplicate systems are the biggest adoption risk. If the estimator keeps a personal spreadsheet, the CRM will become stale. If the office writes callbacks on paper, the task list will be ignored. Pick the system of record first, train to that workflow, and add automations only after the team is entering the basic data consistently. Review that process every Friday until the team trusts the data.</p>

## FAQ

### Do contractors need a CRM or field service software?

<p>Many small contractors need field service software with CRM features first. That keeps follow-up connected to scheduling, invoices, payments, and job history. A standalone CRM makes sense when sales is complex enough to deserve its own process.</p>

### Is HubSpot enough for a contractor?

<p>HubSpot can be enough for basic lead tracking, especially because the free CRM gives small teams a no-card starting point. It is not enough if the same system also needs to dispatch techs, collect payments, or manage field work.</p>

### What CRM should a residential service contractor test first?

<p>Jobber and Housecall Pro should usually be tested first. Jobber is cleaner for small field service workflow, while Housecall Pro is stronger when lead tracking, online booking, homeowner communication, and reviews matter more.</p>

### When should a contractor pay for Pipeline CRM?

<p>Pipeline CRM makes sense when sales has its own stages, owners, and reporting separate from field operations. Commercial contractors, restoration companies with account reps, and firms with dedicated sales staff are better candidates than solo operators.</p>

### What is the biggest CRM buying mistake for contractors?

<p>The biggest mistake is buying a CRM without a follow-up process. Software cannot fix unclear ownership. Each lead needs a status, owner, next step, and review rhythm before the tool can improve close rate.</p>
