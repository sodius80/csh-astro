- q: What is the best CRM for small contractors?
  a: Jobber is the best first look for many small service contractors because client history,
    quotes, jobs, invoices, and follow-up live in the same workflow. Housecall Pro is a strong

- q: Should a contractor use HubSpot or a field service CRM?
  a: Use HubSpot when sales is separate from field operations or the company needs a free
    standalone CRM to start. Use Jobber, Housecall Pro, or ServiceTitan when leads need to
    move directly into scheduling, dispatch, invoices, and field communication.
- q: When is Pipeline CRM a better choice?
  a: Pipeline CRM is better when a contractor has a dedicated sales team, commercial pipeline,
    repeat account work, or a sales process that should stay separate from field scheduling.
- q: Does ServiceTitan include CRM features?
  a: Yes, but its CRM value is tied to a larger field service operating system. It is strongest
    for call booking, customer history, campaign attribution, marketing automation, and booked-revenue
    reporting in larger shops.
- q: What CRM features matter most for contractors?
  a: The most important features are quote follow-up, lead-source tracking, customer history,
    pipeline visibility, reminders, mobile access, reporting, and a clean handoff from sold
    work to scheduled work.
- q: What should contractors avoid when buying CRM software?
  a: Avoid buying a standalone CRM if nobody will maintain it. A CRM only works when every
    lead has an owner, every quote has a next step, and the team reviews stalled opportunities
    every week.

<p>Most contractors ignore CRM until a good quote goes cold because nobody called back, or the owner realizes nobody can say which lead source brings in good work. A basic CRM should do two jobs first: make follow-up hard to miss and show where revenue is coming from.</p>

<p>If your shortlist is already down to a customer-automation platform and a standalone sales CRM, read the focused <a href="/keap-vs-pipeline-crm/">Keap vs Pipeline CRM</a> comparison next.</p>

<p><strong>Disclosure:</strong> Some links on this page are affiliate links. If you sign up through one, I earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. ContractorSoftwareHub's recommendations do not change based on that.</p>

<p><strong>Right for:</strong> Service contractors losing jobs to missed follow-ups or guessing which lead sources produce the best work. Jobber's built-in CRM features cover the first problem for many small shops. Housecall Pro or ServiceTitan makes more sense when pipeline visibility and attribution are bigger concerns.</p>

<p><strong>Not for:</strong> Contractors running a complex B2B sales cycle with large commercial accounts and several decision-makers on each deal. That kind of selling needs a purpose-built sales CRM, not CRM features attached to field service software.</p>

## Do You Need This Yet?

CRM software pays for itself when quote volume gets high enough that memory stops being a system. Two quick checks:

- **You don't need it yet** if you send a handful of quotes a month and can remember every callback without a list. Your phone's notes app still works at that scale.
- **You need it now** if jobs have slipped away because nobody followed up, or you cannot tell which lead source produces revenue.

If you're in the middle - growing, but not buried in leads - Jobber is the safer first test. The built-in quote follow-up automation on Connect and higher plans cuts the risk that unanswered quotes disappear into a notebook or inbox while you figure out what else you need.

## Pricing Reality for Contractor CRM Buyers

<p>CRM pricing looks simple until the contractor asks where sales work ends and field work begins. Jobber and Housecall Pro bundle CRM features into a broader field service workflow. For a small service company, that usually beats paying for a separate CRM and then copying job details into scheduling software. Jobber starts at $29/month on annual billing, while Housecall Pro starts at $59/month on annual billing.</p>

<p>Pipedrive and Pipeline CRM make more sense when sales can live outside field service. Pipedrive starts at $14/seat/month on annual billing, making it the most affordable dedicated sales CRM. Pipeline CRM starts at $25/user/month on annual billing and offers deeper deal management and reporting. HubSpot has a no-cost CRM for up to two users, then paid Sales Hub seats start around $7/month per seat on annual billing. Freshsales and Zoho CRM both offer no-cost tiers for up to 3 users; Freshsales adds built-in phone and AI lead scoring, while Zoho CRM adds custom modules.</p>

<p>JobNimbus sits in a different bucket: contractor-specific CRM with custom quote pricing. The public plan structure is Essentials, Pro, Premium, and Enterprise, but the vendor does not publish dollar rates. That makes it harder to compare on spreadsheet price, but easier to justify when roofing-specific boards, measurements, supplier ordering, photo workflows, and QuickBooks matter more than a low per-seat CRM rate.</p>

<p>Keap bundles CRM with email marketing starting at $299/mo billed monthly for 2 users and 1,500 contacts. FollowUp CRM's pricing is quote-based across all three tiers (Essentials, Professional, Enterprise) with no published per-user rates. Builder Prime uses request-based pricing for both Growth and Multiply plans. TopBuilder's pricing is also request-only, so get a written quote before comparing it against sales CRMs with published seats.</p>

<p>ServiceTitan should not be treated as a cheap CRM upgrade. Its pricing is quote-based and tied to per-technician assumptions, package selection, implementation, and a larger operating platform. It belongs in the CRM shortlist when campaign tracking, call booking, customer history, and booked-revenue reporting are management-level problems, not when a two-tech shop simply needs quote reminders.</p>

## CRM Demo Checklist for Contractors

<p>Run the CRM demo with one real lead source. Ask the vendor to create a new lead from a phone call, website form, referral, or paid ad. Then have them assign an owner, send a quote, schedule follow-up, mark the quote won or lost, and show where the revenue source appears in reporting.</p>

<p>For Jobber and Housecall Pro, watch the handoff. When a quote is accepted, does the job move naturally into scheduling, customer communication, invoice, and payment collection? If a contractor still needs to retype the same information into another tool, the CRM benefit is smaller than the demo makes it look.</p>

<p>For Pipedrive, Pipeline CRM, HubSpot, Freshsales, and Zoho CRM, test sales discipline. Can the team build stages that match the real sales process? Can users see stale deals, overdue tasks, next follow-up date, lead source, and expected value without building custom reports from scratch? If the sales manager cannot review the pipeline in 15 minutes on Monday morning, the CRM will not change behavior.</p>

## Where Each CRM Is a Wrong Fit

<p><strong>Jobber</strong> is the wrong fit if the company needs a complex standalone sales pipeline with account management, commercial deal stages, and sales-only reporting. It works best when CRM sits inside field service operations.</p>

<p><strong>Housecall Pro</strong> is the wrong fit if the buyer only wants a cheap contact database. Its value shows up when pipeline, online booking, reminders, payments, reviews, and homeowner communication all matter.</p>

<p><strong>Pipeline CRM</strong> is the wrong fit if the office wants one system for dispatch and invoicing. It is a sales CRM, so the contractor still needs a clear handoff into field operations.</p>

<p><strong>Pipedrive</strong> is the wrong fit if you need field service, dispatch, invoicing, or project management. It is a sales CRM only. It is also the wrong fit if you need a no-cost forever tier - the 14-day trial converts to $14/seat/mo. Look at Freshsales or HubSpot's free CRM instead.</p>

<p><strong>HubSpot</strong> is the wrong fit if the contractor expects it to manage crews, appointments, invoices, or job costing. It is a good free CRM starting point, not field service software.</p>

<p><strong>Freshsales</strong> is the wrong fit if the team needs workflow automation, email cadences, or more than 1 third-party integration on the free tier. It is also not field service software - no scheduling, dispatch, or invoicing.</p>

<p><strong>Zoho CRM</strong> is the wrong fit if the team is larger than 3 people and needs more than 5,000 records on the free tier, or if the contractor wants a simpler setup without navigating custom modules and fields.</p>

<p><strong>JobNimbus</strong> is the wrong fit if the company is not centered on roofing, siding, gutters, or exterior restoration. Its trade-specific workflow is the point. Mixed-trade service teams that mostly need scheduling and invoices should compare Jobber or Housecall Pro first.</p>

<p><strong>Keap</strong> is the wrong fit if you only need a simple contact list or your operation runs entirely through field service software. The extra automation features add complexity that a basic CRM user won't need.</p>

<p><strong>ServiceTitan</strong> is the wrong fit for small shops that need reminders and a simple pipeline. It is built for larger field service operations where marketing attribution and booked revenue justify the implementation effort.</p>

<p><strong>FollowUp CRM</strong> is the wrong fit if your team needs field production tools, native mobile apps, or published pricing before talking to sales. It is a pre-construction sales CRM, not an all-in-one platform.</p>

<p><strong>Builder Prime</strong> is the wrong fit for general contractors outside home improvement, solo operators who need a lighter tool, or anyone who requires transparent subscription pricing before a demo conversation.</p>

<p><strong>TopBuilder</strong> is the wrong fit for small residential contractors under five people, teams that want an all-in-one platform with project management, or buyers who rely on abundant independent reviews before committing.</p>

## Implementation Plan for Better Follow-Up

<p>The first CRM project should be smaller than most buyers expect. Do not start with every feature. Start with the follow-up rule: every quote needs an owner, a status, a next action, and a due date. If those four fields are maintained, the CRM will prevent most lost follow-up before any advanced automation is added.</p>

<p>Week one should define stages such as new lead, appointment scheduled, quote sent, follow-up due, won, lost, and dormant. Week two should connect quote templates, customer communication, and basic reporting. Week three should review every lead older than seven days. Week four should compare lead source to booked work and decide which reports the owner will use.</p>

<p>Duplicate systems are the biggest adoption risk. If the estimator keeps a personal spreadsheet, the CRM will become stale. If the office writes callbacks on paper, the task list will be ignored. Pick the system of record first, train to that workflow, and add automations only after the team is entering the basic data consistently. Review that process every Friday until the team trusts the data.</p>

## Bottom Line

<p>If you're running a small service operation and jobs are slipping because follow-up is loose, <strong>Jobber's</strong> built-in client history and quote follow-up features may solve the problem without adding another system.</p>

<p>If inbound leads are coming from several sources and you need better visibility into what converts, <strong>Housecall Pro's</strong> Pipeline and lead-source tools are the stronger fit.</p>

<p>Choose <strong>Pipedrive</strong> if you want the most affordable standalone sales CRM at $14/seat/mo with a 14-day no-card trial. Choose <strong>Pipeline CRM</strong> if you have a dedicated sales operation separate from field work and need deeper pipeline management. Choose <strong>HubSpot</strong> if you want to start structured lead management at no cost. Choose <strong>Freshsales</strong> if a tiny team wants a no-cost CRM with built-in phone and AI lead scoring. Choose <strong>Zoho CRM</strong> if you need custom fields and modules to model contractor-specific data or want to grow into the Zoho ecosystem. Choose <strong>JobNimbus</strong> if roofing, siding, gutters, or exterior restoration are the core business and the CRM needs to connect sales boards, production stages, measurements, supplier ordering, field photos, and QuickBooks.</p>

<p>Consider <strong>FollowUp CRM</strong> for construction-specific bid tracking when your team has a genuine pre-construction sales pipeline problem. Consider <strong>Builder Prime</strong> if you are a home improvement contractor who wants CRM, estimating, and production in one platform. Consider <strong>TopBuilder</strong> if you are a specialty commercial contractor who needs ERP-connected bid scoring and financial dashboards. Consider <strong>ServiceTitan</strong> when you're managing a large operation and need campaign ROI tied to booked revenue.</p>
