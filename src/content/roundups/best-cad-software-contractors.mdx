<p>Most contractors don't need full CAD software. What they actually need is something that produces a drawing a permit office will accept, something that communicates scope clearly to subs and clients, and something they can learn without a semester of training. That's a much shorter list of requirements than most CAD comparison articles assume.</p>

<p><strong>Quick answer:</strong> Start with the drawing bottleneck you actually have right now — .DWG submissions, client-facing 3D visuals, residential design documents, or low-cost 2D drafting — not the platform with the longest CAD feature list.</p>

## Do You Need This Yet?

CAD software pays for itself when you're doing enough volume that manual drafting and permit submittals start eating into billable hours. Two quick checks:

- **You don't need it yet** if you're only doing a few small residential jobs a year and your municipality accepts hand sketches or basic PDFs. A decent tablet and a free sketching app still works fine at that scale.
- **You need it now** if your building department or commercial GCs require .DWG files, or if you're spending half a day on drawings that should take an hour.

If you're in the middle—growing but not buried in drawing work yet—start with the deliverable your clients actually require. AutoCAD LT is the safest default when a client, GC, or permit workflow specifically requires native DWG files; SketchUp or DraftSight may be enough when visuals or low-cost 2D drafting are the real bottleneck.

## Product Reviews

### 1. AutoCAD LT – Best for Contractors Who Need .DWG Compatibility

<p>Autodesk currently shows AutoCAD LT at $70/month or $540/year and full AutoCAD at $260/month or $2,095/year before taxes and regional cart changes. LT is the 2D drafting version; full AutoCAD adds 3D CAD and specialized toolsets most small contractors will not use.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> AutoCAD LT creates DWG files for AutoCAD-centered workflows and native DWG deliverables. It is the safest default when a client, GC, or permitting workflow specifically requires DWG-native submissions.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> The learning curve is steep. Budget real training time before relying on it for deadline-sensitive drawings; for someone running a small crew, that is a hard sell.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> AutoCAD LT is currently shown at $70/month or $540/year before taxes and regional cart changes. Full AutoCAD is shown at $260/month or $2,095/year.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors doing commercial work or submitting to municipalities that require .DWG format. Residential-only contractors can probably skip it.</p>

### 2. SketchUp – Best for 3D Visualization and Client Proposals

<p>SketchUp is not really a CAD tool in the traditional sense. It will not produce permit-ready construction documents on its own. What it does well is 3D visualization that clients can understand, especially for design-build work or renovation proposals where a model sells scope better than a flat sketch.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> SketchUp is positioned around essential 3D design on web and iPad, with Pro adding desktop modeling and documentation. Go is lower-cost for visuals; Pro is the better fit when LayOut and desktop workflow matter.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Producing actual construction documents usually requires SketchUp Pro with LayOut or exporting into a separate drawing tool. If trades need formal CAD/BIM deliverables, plan on Pro/LayOut or CAD export rather than relying on Go alone.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> SketchUp Go Annual is $10.75/month per user billed annually, or $129/year equivalent; Go Monthly is $19.99/month. SketchUp Pro Annual is $33.25/month per user billed annually, or $399/year equivalent; Pro Monthly is $99.99/month.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who need to show clients what a project will look like. Not a replacement for proper construction documents on its own.</p>

### 3. Chief Architect – Best for Residential Remodelers and Custom Home Builders

<p>Chief Architect Premier is one of the higher-priced tools here at $229/month or $1,995/year annually, and it is the one most specialized for residential design-build. If you are doing custom homes, additions, or significant remodels and you need construction documents, material lists, and client presentations from the same model, this is the tool built around that workflow.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> The automation. Where AutoCAD requires you to draft everything manually, Chief Architect generates floor plans, elevations, and 3D views from the same model. Update the model and the drawings update.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> The price is hard to justify for occasional use. It makes more sense when residential design, construction documents, material lists, and client presentations are regular work.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Chief Architect Premier is $1,995/year billed annually ($166.25/month equivalent) or $229/month.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Residential remodelers and custom home builders doing complex projects regularly. Too much for occasional drawing needs.</p>

### 4. DraftSight – Best Budget Option for AutoCAD-Compatible Drawings

<p>DraftSight Professional starts at $299/year, Premium is $599/year, and Network options start at $399/year before any cart promotions. Professional creates, views, and edits DWG, DXF, and DGN files, which keeps it compatible with AutoCAD-centered 2D drafting workflows.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> DraftSight Professional creates, views, and edits DWG, DXF, and DGN files for AutoCAD-centered 2D workflows. The subscription is still materially cheaper than AutoCAD LT, though the gap is not as wide as older pricing made it look.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Professional is the lower-cost 2D option, while Premium, Network, and Enterprise tiers add capabilities and licensing/support options. Verify the current cart promo, support terms, and whether Premium's 3D/BIM features matter before buying.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> DraftSight Professional starts at $299/year; Premium is $599/year; Network options start at $399/year. The current page notes a cart promo, so verify checkout pricing.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who need .DWG compatibility without AutoCAD's price tag. Good for 2D construction drawings and permit submissions.</p>

### 5. ProgeCAD – Best One-Time Purchase

<p>progeCAD fills a specific niche: contractors who want a perpetual DWG-compatible CAD license instead of another annual subscription. progeSOFT positions progeCAD Professional around perpetual licensing with no annual fee and a 30-day trial, but current U.S. cart pricing is not consistently exposed on the product page, so confirm the reseller/cart price before budgeting.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> progeCAD works natively with DWG and is positioned as AutoCAD-compatible. It offers AutoCAD-like Classic and Ribbon interfaces with compatible commands.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Official pages emphasize perpetual licensing more than transparent U.S. cart pricing. Before relying on it for deadline-critical work, verify reseller support, maintenance terms, and available training resources.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Perpetual license; verify current reseller/cart price before purchase.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who prefer a perpetual DWG-compatible CAD license and are comfortable verifying reseller/support terms.</p>

## Common Questions

### Do contractors actually need CAD software?

<p>Most residential contractors running small crews do not need it for routine work. Where it becomes necessary: permit applications that require proper drawing files, projects involving other trades who expect drawing packages, and design-build work where a hand sketch does not meet client or structural expectations. If none of those situations apply, skip the overhead.</p>

### What drawing format do permit offices accept?

<p>It depends on your jurisdiction. Many municipal building departments now accept PDF drawings at specified scale, which any of these tools can produce. Some commercial projects and larger municipalities specifically require .DWG files. Before buying anything, call your local building department and ask what they actually accept.</p>

## Bottom Line

<p>If you are doing commercial work or submitting to a client, GC, or municipality that specifically requires native DWG files, AutoCAD LT is the safest default. Budget the time to learn it properly.</p>

<p>If you're doing residential design-build at any real volume, Chief Architect earns its cost through time savings on permit documents and material takeoffs.</p>

<p>If you just need to show clients what a project will look like, SketchUp Go Annual at $129/year equivalent is the fastest path to a presentable visual. Pair it with DraftSight Professional for 2D permit drawings and you have covered both bases for about $428/year before taxes and cart changes.</p>
