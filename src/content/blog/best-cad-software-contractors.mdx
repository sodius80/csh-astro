<p><strong>Quick answer:</strong> Start with the tool that matches the bottleneck you actually have right now — sales follow-up, dispatch, job costing, proposal speed, or accounting visibility — not the platform with the longest feature list.</p>

## Product Reviews

### 1. AutoCAD LT – Best for Contractors Who Need .DWG Compatibility

<p>AutoCAD LT runs $55/month or $440/year through Autodesk's site. That's the 2D-only version. The full AutoCAD (with 3D) runs $220/month, which very few contractors need. If you're looking at full AutoCAD pricing and wondering why it's that expensive, the answer is you're paying for features you won't use.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> It produces .DWG files, which is the format municipal permit offices and commercial GCs expect. If you're doing any commercial work or your municipality specifically requires .DWG submissions, you're going to end up here eventually.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> The learning curve. It takes three to six months before you're producing drawings at a pace that makes the tool feel worth it. For someone running a small crew, that's a hard sell.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> $440/year (LT, 2D only) | $2,640/year (full AutoCAD).</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors doing commercial work or submitting to municipalities that require .DWG format. Residential-only contractors can probably skip it.</p>

### 2. SketchUp – Best for 3D Visualization and Client Proposals

<p>SketchUp is not really a CAD tool in the traditional sense. It won't produce permit-ready construction documents on its own. What it does well is 3D visualization that clients can actually understand, which has real value if you're doing any kind of design-build work or renovation where showing the client what they're getting matters.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> The learning curve is genuinely easier than AutoCAD. Multiple contractors note they were producing usable 3D models within a week or two.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Producing actual permit drawings still requires either SketchUp Pro with LayOut, or exporting into a separate drawing tool. If your workflow requires sharing files with other trades, that's a real friction point.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Free (web, limited) | $119/year (Go) | $349/year (Pro).</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who need to show clients what a project will look like. Not a replacement for proper construction documents on its own.</p>

### 3. Chief Architect – Best for Residential Remodelers and Custom Home Builders

<p>Chief Architect is the most expensive tool here at $199/month or $2,595/year, and it's the one most specialized for a specific use case: residential design-build. If you're doing custom homes, additions, or significant remodels and you need to produce permit drawings, material takeoffs, and client presentations all from the same model, this is the tool that actually integrates all of that.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> The automation. Where AutoCAD requires you to draft everything manually, Chief Architect generates floor plans, elevations, and 3D views from the same model. Update the model and the drawings update.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> The price is hard to justify for occasional use. "If you're doing five or more projects a year with real design complexity, the time it saves pays for itself. If you're doing one or two simple jobs, you're overpaying."</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> $2,595/year (Premier) | $199/month.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Residential remodelers and custom home builders doing complex projects regularly. Too much for occasional drawing needs.</p>

### 4. DraftSight – Best Budget Option for AutoCAD-Compatible Drawings

<p>DraftSight Professional runs $149/year. It reads and writes .DWG files natively, which means it's compatible with everything AutoCAD produces. For a contractor who needs to work in .DWG format but can't justify $440/year for AutoCAD LT, DraftSight is the most direct alternative.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> The interface is close enough to AutoCAD that anyone who has used AutoCAD can get productive in DraftSight quickly. The cost is roughly a third of AutoCAD LT.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Polish and support. The UI feels dated compared to AutoCAD, and bug fixes come slower. "AutoCAD from five years ago at a third of the price – which is a good deal if you can live with a tool that's a generation behind."</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> $149/year (Professional).</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who need .DWG compatibility without AutoCAD's price tag. Good for 2D construction drawings and permit submissions.</p>

### 5. ProgeCAD – Best One-Time Purchase

<p>ProgeCAD is the least well-known tool on this list but it fills a specific niche: contractors who want to own their software rather than rent it. ProgeCAD Professional runs around $499 as a one-time purchase. After that, you own it.</p>

<p><strong>What stands out:</strong> Like DraftSight, it's AutoCAD-compatible and reads .DWG files. The interface is closer to older versions of AutoCAD than the current interface, which some experienced AutoCAD users actually prefer.</p>

<p><strong>Where it falls short:</strong> Customer support is slower than the major vendors, the interface hasn't kept pace with modern design conventions, and finding community help is harder. If you run into a technical problem on a deadline, you're more likely to be waiting on ProgeCAD's support.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> ~$499 one-time (Professional).</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Contractors who want to own their software and do occasional 2D drafting work. Not the right call if you're producing drawings weekly.</p>

## Common Questions

### Do contractors actually need CAD software?

<p>Most residential contractors running small crews do not need it for routine work. Where it becomes necessary: permit applications that require proper drawing files, projects involving other trades who expect drawing packages, and design-build work where a hand sketch does not meet client or structural expectations. If none of those situations apply, skip the overhead.</p>

### What drawing format do permit offices accept?

<p>It depends on your jurisdiction. Many municipal building departments now accept PDF drawings at specified scale, which any of these tools can produce. Some commercial projects and larger municipalities specifically require .DWG files. Before buying anything, call your local building department and ask what they actually accept.</p>

## Bottom Line

<p>If you're doing commercial work or submitting to municipalities that require .DWG format, AutoCAD LT is probably unavoidable. Budget the time to learn it properly.</p>

<p>If you're doing residential design-build at any real volume, Chief Architect earns its cost through time savings on permit documents and material takeoffs.</p>

<p>If you just need to show clients what a project will look like, SketchUp Go at $119/year is the fastest path to a presentable visual. Pair it with DraftSight for the actual permit drawings and you've covered both bases for about $270/year total.</p>
