## What Jobber Gets Right

<p>The scheduling and dispatch workflow is genuinely well designed. The calendar gives you a full view of your team's day, you can drag jobs to different techs or time slots, and customers automatically get notified when their tech is heading over. For a residential service contractor, this eliminates a real category of daily friction.</p>

<p>Multiple G2 reviewers from HVAC and plumbing companies specifically call out the automated customer notifications as the feature that changed their operation most. One reviewer running a 7-tech plumbing company described it plainly: "Before Jobber, half my morning was calling customers to tell them when the tech was coming. Now it's automatic. I don't think about it."</p>

<p>The quoting flow is also well executed. Build a line-item quote, send it by text or email, customer approves and signs digitally, and it converts to a job without you touching anything. The automated quote follow-up (a reminder text goes out if the quote hasn't been accepted after a few days) is underrated — multiple contractors report winning jobs they had assumed were dead because of that automated nudge.</p>

<p>The mobile app is reliable. Techs can view job details, upload photos, log time, add materials, and collect payment in the field. The offline mode works reasonably well — jobs sync when connectivity returns. This matters more than software companies want to admit, because a lot of contractors work in basements, rural areas, and buildings with spotty signal.</p>

## Where Jobber Falls Short

<p>The biggest gap is job costing. Jobber tracks time and materials, but it doesn't give you real profitability analysis by job, by technician, or by service type. For a contractor running $500K in revenue with tight margins, that visibility matters. If you're past that scale and don't know which jobs are actually profitable, Jobber won't tell you.</p>

<p>The multi-phase estimate functionality is also limited. If you're doing construction-style work where projects break into distinct phases with separate budgets and timelines, Jobber doesn't handle that cleanly. You end up creating separate jobs for each phase, which fragments the client relationship and makes reporting messy.</p>

<p>Reporting is adequate on the Core and Connect tiers but becomes more meaningful on Grow ($199/mo). For a small shop, the basic reports are usually enough. Once you're analyzing crew productivity, job profitability by service type, or material cost trends, you start feeling the limits.</p>

<p>The pricing jump from Connect ($149/mo) to Grow ($199/mo) is steep for what you get. Multiple reviewers mention feeling pushed toward Grow because specific features they need are gated there, but the $50/mo increase doesn't feel proportional to the added value.</p>
